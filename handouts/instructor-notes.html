Module 1

Know What You Have

10 Slides — Pension · VA Disability · TSP · Social Security · Survivor Benefits

Slide 1 of 10

How Your Pension Works

Key Facts & Numbers

  • High-3 formula: 2.5% × years of service × average of highest 36 months' base pay
  • Blended Retirement System (BRS): 2.0% × years of service + TSP matching up to 5%
  • Retirement pay is taxable federal income; state taxation varies (see Module 4)
  • COLA adjustments happen annually in January based on the Consumer Price Index (CPI)

How to Explain It

  • Open with: "Before you can optimize your retirement, you need to know exactly what's coming in each month. Let's start with the foundation — your military pension."
  • Use the formula visually: "If you served 20 years under High-3 and your top 36-month average pay was $5,000/month, that's 50% of $5,000 = $2,500/month for life."
  • Emphasize: BRS members get a smaller multiplier but the TSP match helps close the gap — both matter.
  • Note that the pension is a lifetime guaranteed annuity — no market risk, no running out of money.

Anticipated Questions

Q: What if I retired early (before 20 years on a medical discharge)?
A: PDRL (Permanent Disability Retirement List) and TDRL (Temporary) have different calculations. Refer them to their branch retirement services office for their specific situation.
Q: Can my pension be reduced?
A: The pension itself is protected, but VA disability compensation can affect taxable vs. non-taxable portions. We cover the VA waiver in Module 2.

Slide 2 of 10

Reading Your Pension Statement

Key Facts & Numbers

  • myPay (DFAS) is the primary portal for active/recent retirees
  • The Leave and Earnings Statement (LES) shows gross pay, deductions, SBP premium, allotments, and net
  • SBP premiums appear as a deduction at 6.5% of elected base amount
  • Federal tax withholding is listed separately; VA waiver offset (if applicable) shows as a reduction to gross

How to Explain It

  • Walk participants through the LES layout: gross → deductions → net.
  • Point out where SBP appears so they can confirm they're enrolled (or not).
  • Encourage everyone to log into myPay this week and verify their withholding elections are current.
  • Mention that DFAS Customer Service (1-888-332-7411) can help with statement questions.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Why does my net pay seem lower than expected?
A: Common culprits: SBP premium, federal withholding, allotments, or a VA offset. Walk through the LES line by line with them during a break if needed.
Q: How do I change my withholding?
A: Log into myPay.dfas.mil and update your W-4 elections. Changes take effect the next pay cycle.

Slide 3 of 10

VA Disability Compensation

Key Facts & Numbers

  • VA compensation is tax-free federal income, paid separate from military retirement pay
  • Ratings: 0%–100% in increments of 10% (combined ratings use VA's "whole person" math)
  • A 100% P&T (Permanent & Total) rating opens additional benefits: CHAMPVA for dependents, property tax exemptions in many states
  • COLA on VA comp is applied separately from retirement pay COLA, also tied to CPI

How to Explain It

  • Clarify the common confusion: VA compensation and retirement pay are two separate streams of income.
  • Explain "combined rating" math briefly — it's not additive. Two 50% ratings ≠ 100%. The VA applies each to the "remaining" whole person.
  • Reinforce: if anyone in the room is not yet rated or thinks their rating is too low, Module 2 covers how to appeal and file for increases.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Can I have both retirement pay and VA comp?
A: Yes — but a dollar-for-dollar offset applies unless you qualify for CRDP or CRSC. That's the core topic of Module 2.
Q: Is my VA rating permanent?
A: Not automatically. Ratings can be reduced if the VA re-examines and finds improvement. P&T ratings are protected from reductions. Encourage them to request P&T designation if their conditions are permanent.

Slide 4 of 10

Your Retirement Savings Account

Key Facts & Numbers

  • TSP is the federal government's 401(k)-equivalent — same contribution limits
  • Traditional TSP: pre-tax contributions, taxable on withdrawal
  • Roth TSP: after-tax contributions, tax-free qualified withdrawals
  • Five core investment funds: G (government securities), F (bonds), C (large-cap), S (small-cap), I (international)
  • Lifecycle (L) Funds auto-adjust allocation based on target retirement date

How to Explain It

  • Many retirees haven't touched their TSP in years. This slide is a prompt to log back in.
  • Explain: after separation, contributions stop, but the money stays in TSP and continues to grow tax-deferred.
  • They have three options after retirement: leave it in TSP, roll it over to an IRA/Roth IRA, or withdraw it (subject to taxes and potentially penalties).
  • TSP.gov has a "TSP Calculators" tool they can use to model withdrawals.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Should I roll over my TSP to an IRA?
A: Neither option is universally better. TSP has very low expense ratios (often under 0.05%), which is hard to beat. IRAs offer more investment flexibility. Encourage them to consult a fee-only financial advisor. Don't give a recommendation.
Q: Can I still contribute to TSP after I retire?
A: Only if you return to federal civilian employment. Military retirees who are no longer federal employees cannot make new TSP contributions.

Slide 5 of 10

Knowing Your Plan

Key Facts & Numbers

  • This slide covers the action checklist: confirm enrollment, verify beneficiaries, understand allocation
  • TSP beneficiary designations are managed separately from a will — they supersede the will
  • Many veterans have outdated beneficiary designations (ex-spouses, deceased relatives)

How to Explain It

  • Pause here and ask: "When did you last log in to TSP.gov and check your beneficiary?" Most people haven't done it in years.
  • Stress that beneficiary designations on TSP and life insurance policies override whatever a will says. This is one of the most impactful 5-minute actions they can take today.
  • Reference the Beneficiary Designation Checklist handout distributed with the course.
Facilitation Tip: Ask for a show of hands — "How many of you have updated your TSP beneficiary in the last 3 years?" Use the response to create urgency without embarrassing anyone.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Can I name a trust as a TSP beneficiary?
A: Yes, but it's complex. The trust must be properly structured to receive qualified plan assets. Recommend they consult an estate attorney (Module 5 covers this more).

Slide 6 of 10

Withdrawals & Required Minimum Distributions

Key Facts & Numbers

  • RMD Age: 73 if born 1951–1959; 75 if born 1960 or later (SECURE 2.0 Act)
  • Penalty for missing RMD: 25% of the amount that should have been withdrawn (reduced to 10% if corrected promptly)
  • RMDs apply to Traditional TSP and Traditional IRA; NOT to Roth IRA (Roth TSP now also exempt from RMDs)
  • Early withdrawal (before 59½): 10% penalty + income taxes, with exceptions (disability, substantially equal periodic payments)

How to Explain It

  • Many attendees may be approaching or past RMD age. This is high-urgency, high-stakes content.
  • Walk through the timeline: at your RMD age, the IRS requires you to start withdrawing a minimum amount each year based on your account balance and life expectancy factor.
  • Emphasize: TSP will send a notice, but the responsibility to withdraw is on the account holder. Missing an RMD is one of the most expensive tax mistakes a retiree can make.
  • TSP.gov provides an RMD calculator. IRS Publication 590-B covers the full schedule.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Do I have to take my RMD all at once?
A: No. You can take multiple distributions throughout the year as long as the total meets the minimum by December 31 (or April 1 of the following year for the first RMD).
Q: What if I have multiple accounts — TSP and an IRA?
A: Each account type has its own RMD calculation. IRAs can be aggregated (take the total from any one IRA), but TSP RMDs must be taken separately from TSP.

Slide 7 of 10

Social Security for Veterans

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Full Retirement Age (FRA): 67 for anyone born in 1960 or later; 66y2mo–66y10mo for those born 1955–1959
  • Claiming early (age 62): benefit is permanently reduced ~6.67% per year, up to 30% total reduction
  • Delaying past FRA: benefit grows 8% per year up to age 70 — the maximum claiming age
  • Veterans receive Social Security credits for active-duty service (special credits 1957–2001)
  • Military retirement pay does NOT reduce Social Security benefits — they are fully independent

How to Explain It

  • Address the #1 myth immediately: "Your military pension does NOT reduce your Social Security. They are completely separate. You earned both."
  • The claiming age decision is one of the biggest financial decisions retirees make. Early claiming locks in a lower benefit for life; delay builds in permanent raises.
  • For married couples, the higher-earning spouse's claiming strategy affects survivor income — delay can protect the surviving spouse.
  • Encourage everyone to create a my Social Security account at ssa.gov to see their estimated benefits.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Does the VA Waiver affect Social Security?
A: No. VA disability compensation has no impact on Social Security whatsoever. They are entirely separate federal programs.
Q: What if I'm still working — can I collect SS early?
A: If you're under FRA and working, your SS benefit is temporarily reduced if earnings exceed the annual exempt amount ($22,320 in 2024). After reaching FRA, there's no earnings test.

Slide 8 of 10

Spousal & Survivor Benefits

Key Facts & Numbers

  • A spouse can claim Social Security on the retiree's record: up to 50% of the retiree's FRA benefit
  • Survivor benefit (after retiree's death): up to 100% of the benefit the retiree was receiving
  • Government Pension Offset (GPO): if the survivor receives a government pension not covered by SS, spousal/survivor SS may be reduced by 2/3 of that pension
  • Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) was repealed effective January 2024 (Social Security Fairness Act) — veterans with mixed service records should re-evaluate their SS estimates

How to Explain It

  • Spousal coordination is complex. The goal is for the couple to maximize combined lifetime income, not just each individual benefit.
  • A common strategy: lower-earning spouse claims early to bring in income; higher earner delays to build up the survivor benefit.
  • Mention the WEP repeal — this is recent and very relevant. Veterans affected by WEP (those with non-covered pensions) may see their SS benefit increase starting 2024. They should check ssa.gov for recalculations.

Anticipated Questions

Q: What happens to my spouse's SS income when I die?
A: Your spouse keeps the higher of the two benefits — theirs or yours (as survivor). They lose the lower one. This is why the higher earner delaying can protect the surviving spouse's income significantly.
Q: Does SBP affect Social Security survivor benefits?
A: No. SBP (Survivor Benefit Plan from military retirement) and Social Security survivor benefits are completely separate and both can be received simultaneously.

Slide 9 of 10

Social Security — The Dollar Math

Key Facts & Numbers

  • SS benefit is calculated from your 35 highest-earning years, indexed for inflation
  • The Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) is the benefit at exactly FRA
  • Early reduction: about 6.67%/year for first 3 years before FRA, then 5%/year
  • Delayed credit: 8%/year from FRA to age 70 — a total of 24% more if born 1960+

How to Explain It

  • Walk through a concrete example: "If your FRA benefit is $2,000/month at 67, claiming at 62 gives you about $1,400/month. Waiting to 70 gives you about $2,480/month — permanently."
  • The breakeven point (when total delayed benefits exceed total early benefits) is typically around age 80–82. For those in good health with family longevity, delay usually wins.
  • Reference the SSA's Retirement Estimator at ssa.gov for personalized projections.
Facilitation Tip: This is a great discussion moment. Ask: "How many of you have checked your Social Security statement on ssa.gov?" Encourage everyone who hasn't to do it within the next week. It's free and takes 5 minutes.

Anticipated Questions

Q: What if Social Security runs out of money?
A: Current projections show Social Security's trust fund may be depleted around 2033–2035, at which point benefits could be reduced to ~77–80% of scheduled amounts. However, Congress has historically intervened. This is a planning consideration, not a certainty. Encourage them to plan conservatively.

Module 2

Maximizing Your Disability Benefits

8 Slides — Claims · CRDP/CRSC · VA Waiver · VA Pension · Scams

Slide 1 of 8

Filing New Claims & Rating Increases

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Veterans can file new claims or request re-evaluation of existing conditions at any time
  • The VA rates each condition separately; combined rating uses the "whole person" method (not additive)
  • Effective date of a successful claim is generally the date of application — file sooner to preserve back pay
  • Average VA claims decision time varies; Fully Developed Claims (FDC) can reduce processing time

How to Explain It

  • Many veterans underestimate their rating because they never filed for all their conditions. Every service-connected condition should be on record.
  • Walk through the process: gather service records and medical evidence, file a claim on VA.gov, attend C&P exam if scheduled.
  • VSOs (Veterans Service Organizations) — DAV, VFW, American Legion — provide free claims assistance. Strongly recommend this resource.
  • Remind: filing is free. There's never a fee to submit a VA disability claim directly. (Scams covered in Slide 7.)

Anticipated Questions

Q: I filed before and was denied. Should I try again?
A: Absolutely. Veterans can file supplemental claims with new evidence. Denial is not final. Encourage them to work with a VSO for appeals.

Slide 2 of 8

Secondary Service-Connected Conditions

Key Facts & Numbers

  • A secondary condition is one caused or aggravated by a service-connected primary condition
  • Example: service-connected knee injury → secondary claim for hip, back, or gait problems from compensating
  • Obesity-related conditions, mental health secondary to physical injuries, and sleep apnea secondary to PTSD are common VA secondary claims
  • Lay statements (buddy statements) from family or fellow service members can support secondary claims

How to Explain It

  • This is one of the most underutilized pathways to a higher combined rating.
  • Veterans often don't realize they can claim conditions that developed as a result of another service-connected issue.
  • Encourage them to document the connection with a medical nexus letter from their doctor explaining how Condition B was caused or worsened by Condition A.
Facilitation Tip: Ask: "Does anyone have a service-connected condition that you think might be causing other health problems?" This often sparks discussion and helps participants recognize claims they haven't filed.

Slide 3 of 8 — Discussion

The VA Waiver (Offset)

What This Slide Covers

  • The VA waiver: when a veteran has both retirement pay and VA disability compensation, the VA comp is tax-free — but dollar-for-dollar, it offsets (reduces) retirement pay unless CRDP or CRSC applies
  • This is NOT a penalty — it prevents "double-dipping" between two compensation systems. VA comp is tax-free; retirement pay is taxable. The offset shifts income to the tax-free source.
  • Net effect can be neutral or even positive (depending on tax bracket) — but understanding it prevents confusion

How to Explain It

  • Use a clear example: "If your retirement pay is $2,000/month and VA comp is $800/month, DFAS reduces your retirement check to $1,200. Total income is still $2,800 — but $800 of it is tax-free."
  • Emphasize: the offset is not money lost — it's money reclassified. The real loss would be being taxed on income you don't have to be taxed on.
  • CRDP and CRSC (Slide 4) are the programs that restore retirement pay on top of VA comp for qualifying veterans.

Facilitation Questions

Discussion Prompt: "How many of you have seen your DFAS statement show a reduced retirement pay amount? Let's walk through why that happens and when it can be avoided."

Slide 4 of 8

CRDP vs. CRSC: Side by Side

Key Facts & Numbers

  • CRDP (Concurrent Retirement and Disability Pay): Restores full retirement pay alongside VA comp. Requires VA rating of 50% or higher. Automatic enrollment through DFAS. Taxable.
  • CRSC (Combat-Related Special Compensation): Tax-free replacement pay for combat-related disabilities. Requires VA rating of 10%+ for combat-related conditions. Must apply through your branch of service.
  • Veterans eligible for both must elect one — you cannot receive both simultaneously
  • DFAS conducts an annual election every January; you can change your election annually

How to Explain It

  • Walk through the comparison side by side: CRDP is automatic and taxable; CRSC requires an application but is tax-free for combat-related portions.
  • The right choice depends on the veteran's specific situation — primarily their tax bracket and the nature of their disability.
  • For many veterans at lower income levels, CRSC's tax-free nature makes it more valuable even if the gross amount is lower.
  • The DFAS website (dfas.mil) has a CRDP/CRSC comparison tool. Encourage participants to use it.

Anticipated Questions

Q: I never applied for CRSC. Can I still do it?
A: Yes. Contact your branch of service's CRSC program office. Back pay may be available from the date of your CRSC application, not the date your conditions were rated. Apply as soon as possible.
Q: What does "combat-related" mean for CRSC?
A: It includes direct combat, hazardous duty, armed conflict, and certain training injuries. It does not require being in active combat. Check with your branch's CRSC office for specific determination criteria.

Slide 5 of 8 — Discussion

Annual Election & DFAS Lags

What This Slide Covers

  • CRDP/CRSC elections reset every January — this is the annual opportunity to change your election
  • DFAS processing lags: when a VA rating changes, DFAS may take several months to update payments. Retroactive payments are made once the update is processed.
  • Veterans should monitor both their VA.gov and myPay accounts when expecting a rating change to catch discrepancies early

How to Explain It

  • Frame this as a "do this once a year" action item: every January, log into myPay and confirm your CRDP/CRSC election still makes sense for your situation.
  • Warn about the lag: "If your VA rating increases from 40% to 50%, you become eligible for CRDP. But DFAS won't know until VA notifies them. This can take 2–6 months. Your back pay will come, but you need to watch for it."
  • If there's a discrepancy between what VA says and what DFAS is paying, contact DFAS directly: 1-888-332-7411.
Discussion Prompt: "Has anyone here experienced a delay in their pay after a rating change? What happened?" This usually generates real-world stories that make the content tangible.

Slide 6 of 8

VA Pension & the 3-Year Look-Back

Key Facts & Numbers

  • VA Pension net worth limit: $163,699 (2026) — includes assets and annual income
  • Look-back period: 3 years for asset transfers (gifts, transfers to irrevocable trusts) made to qualify for VA pension
  • Penalty for improper transfers: a period of ineligibility proportional to the value transferred
  • VA Pension is for wartime veterans with limited income/assets who are 65+ or permanently disabled — distinct from VA disability compensation

How to Explain It

  • Clarify the distinction: VA Pension is a needs-based benefit. VA Disability Compensation is earned regardless of financial need.
  • The look-back rule is a trap many families fall into when trying to transfer assets to qualify. Unlike Medicaid (5-year look-back), VA's is 3 years — but the rules are still strict.
  • Caution: there are many predatory "VA pension planning" attorneys and financial advisors who charge large fees to transfer assets. These arrangements can disqualify veterans for 3 years. (This connects directly to Slide 7 on scams.)

Anticipated Questions

Q: I transferred my house to my kids two years ago. Does that count?
A: It depends on whether it was done for VA pension qualification purposes and the terms. This is a complex legal question — recommend they consult a VA-accredited attorney (va.gov/ogc for the accredited attorney list).

Slide 7 of 8

Veteran Benefit Scams: A $584 Million Crisis

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Military consumers (active duty, veterans, family) reported $584 million in total fraud losses (FTC 2024 data)
  • Veterans and retirees specifically reported $419 million in fraud losses (FTC 2024)
  • Most common scam types: impersonation (fake VA/DFAS officials), investment fraud, VA pension "planning" schemes, claims assistance fraud
  • VA claims assistance is free through accredited VSOs — any fee charged to file a basic claim is a red flag

How to Explain It

  • Open with the numbers: "$584 million. That's not a small problem — that's veterans like the people in this room losing money they worked decades to earn."
  • Key warning signs: unsolicited calls/emails about benefits, requests for advance fees, offers to "unlock" hidden benefits, pressure to decide immediately.
  • Legitimate resources that are always free: VA.gov, ssa.gov, accredited VSOs (DAV, VFW, American Legion, MOAA).
  • Report scams to the FTC (reportfraud.ftc.gov), VA OIG (va.gov/oig), or your state attorney general.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Can I hire an attorney to help with my VA claim?
A: Yes, but only after an initial decision has been made. VA-accredited attorneys and claims agents can charge fees for appeal work, but not for initial claims. Check accreditation at va.gov/ogc.
Q: I think I was scammed. What do I do?
A: Report to FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov, the VA OIG at 1-800-488-8244, and your state's consumer protection office. Act quickly — some losses can be recovered if reported promptly.

Module 3

Healthcare & Long-Term Care

9 Slides — CHAMPVA · Medicare · TRICARE · Aid & Attendance

Slide 1 of 9

CHAMPVA: Coverage for Dependents

Key Facts & Numbers

  • CHAMPVA covers: Spouses and dependents of veterans who are 100% P&T rated (or died from a service-connected condition)
  • Coverage level: 75% of allowable charges after the deductible
  • Annual deductible: $50 per person, $100 per family maximum
  • Annual out-of-pocket cap: $3,000 per family
  • CHAMPVA is NOT available to veterans themselves — it's exclusively for eligible dependents
  • CHAMPVA cannot be combined with TRICARE; Medicare-eligible dependents transition from CHAMPVA to Medicare + CHAMPVA supplemental

How to Explain It

  • Clarify the eligibility: CHAMPVA is available to dependents of 100% P&T-rated veterans. If the veteran is rated lower, dependents are not eligible for CHAMPVA.
  • The $50/$100 deductible is among the lowest of any health program — an excellent benefit for qualifying families.
  • After age 65, CHAMPVA-enrolled dependents must enroll in Medicare Parts A & B; Medicare becomes primary and CHAMPVA wraps around it.
  • Apply through the VA Healthcare Eligibility Center: va.gov/health-care/family-caregiver-benefits/champva/

Anticipated Questions

Q: My spouse has employer insurance. Can they also use CHAMPVA?
A: If the employer insurance is other health insurance (OHI), CHAMPVA acts as secondary. CHAMPVA will cover costs the primary insurance doesn't, up to its allowable amounts.

Slide 2 of 9

Medicare at Age 65

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Medicare Part A: Hospital insurance — premium-free for most (if you paid Medicare taxes for 10+ years)
  • Medicare Part B: Medical insurance — standard premium $202.90/month in 2026 (CMS.gov)
  • IRMAA surcharge: Kicks in if income exceeds $106,000 single / $212,000 married filing jointly (2026 thresholds)
  • Enrollment window: 7-month window (3 months before, the month of, and 3 months after your 65th birthday)
  • Late enrollment penalty for Part B: 10% per 12-month period you were eligible but not enrolled

How to Explain It

  • Even if veterans use VA healthcare, they may want Medicare Part B to have civilian provider access and to coordinate with TRICARE for Life.
  • The most expensive Medicare mistake: missing the enrollment window and incurring a permanent late penalty.
  • IRMAA is based on income from 2 years prior — a year with unusually high income (like a TSP withdrawal) can trigger a surcharge 2 years later. Plan ahead.

Anticipated Questions

Q: I use the VA exclusively. Do I still need Medicare?
A: VA coverage doesn't protect against the late enrollment penalty. If you want TRICARE for Life (which wraps around Medicare), you must have Medicare Part B. Most veterans with TFL should enroll in Part B at 65.

Slide 3 of 9

TRICARE for Life

Key Facts & Numbers

  • TRICARE for Life (TFL) is automatic for Medicare-eligible military retirees — no enrollment required, but Medicare Part B must be active
  • TFL acts as a secondary payer: Medicare pays first, TFL covers most remaining costs — effectively zero out-of-pocket for Medicare-covered services
  • TFL does cover some services Medicare does not (e.g., certain overseas care)
  • No annual premium for TFL itself — but Medicare Part B premium ($202.90/month in 2026) is required

How to Explain It

  • TFL is one of the most valuable benefits military retirees have and many don't fully understand it.
  • The key message: "If you have Medicare Part B active, TRICARE for Life is automatic and virtually eliminates your out-of-pocket costs for Medicare-covered services."
  • The cost is essentially the Medicare Part B premium — in return you get near-comprehensive secondary coverage. For most retirees, this is an outstanding deal.

Anticipated Questions

Q: What if I travel internationally? Does TFL work abroad?
A: Yes, TFL has international coverage for urgent/emergency care. TRICARE also has a Overseas Program for routine care at military facilities abroad. Check tricare.mil for details by region.

Slide 4 of 9

Medicare Part B & Part D: Costly Pitfalls

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Part B late penalty: Permanent 10% premium increase per 12-month period without coverage
  • Part D late penalty: 1% per month of the national base beneficiary premium for each month without creditable drug coverage
  • TRICARE and VA drug coverage count as creditable coverage — no Part D penalty while enrolled in either
  • IRMAA for Part D: also income-based surcharge on top of plan premium

How to Explain It

  • The Part B penalty is the bigger trap — it's permanent and compounds. Someone who delays 3 years pays 30% more, forever.
  • Veterans covered by VA pharmacy or TRICARE drug benefits have creditable drug coverage — they're protected from Part D penalties while that coverage is active.
  • Key warning: if they lose TRICARE or VA coverage after 65 and then enroll in Part D, they could still face the penalty for any uncovered months prior to their initial eligibility window.

Anticipated Questions

Q: My spouse turns 65 next year. Should they enroll in Medicare Part B even though I have TRICARE?
A: Yes, if they want TRICARE for Life. TFL requires Medicare Part B for the eligible spouse — not just the veteran. Spouse must enroll independently at 65.

Slide 5 of 9

Coverage Comparison: VA vs. Medicare vs. TRICARE

How to Explain It

  • This comparison slide is a visual anchor — use it to show where each program is primary and where they overlap.
  • Most military retirees over 65 have all three available: VA for service-connected care (and routine care if enrolled), Medicare Part A/B as the civilian primary, and TRICARE for Life as the civilian secondary.
  • Key takeaway: these programs are not mutually exclusive. Veterans can use all three strategically — VA for service-connected conditions and prescriptions, Medicare/TFL for civilian specialists and procedures.
  • TRICARE cannot be used simultaneously with VA care for the same episode of care — you use one or the other for a given service.
Facilitation Tip: Draw a simple three-circle Venn diagram on a whiteboard. Label each circle VA, Medicare, TFL. Show which benefits live exclusively in each and what overlaps. Visuals make this complex topic much more accessible.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Which one should I use for my annual physical?
A: If enrolled in VA healthcare, the VA covers it for free. If seeing a civilian doctor, Medicare is primary and TFL covers most remaining costs. The "best" answer depends on their specific plan and provider preferences.

Slide 6 of 9

VA Aid & Attendance Overview

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Aid & Attendance (A&A) is an enhanced VA pension benefit for veterans who need help with daily living activities
  • Eligibility: wartime veteran, limited income/assets (net worth under $163,699), and meets one of the care criteria
  • Care criteria: needs assistance with 2+ Activities of Daily Living (ADLs), bedridden, in a nursing home, or legally blind
  • A&A benefit is paid on top of basic VA Pension — it's not a separate application; you apply for VA Pension and request A&A consideration

How to Explain It

  • Frame it as a solution to a real problem: "Long-term care is expensive. Home health aides, assisted living, nursing homes — these costs can destroy a retirement savings in months. A&A helps offset that."
  • Important: A&A is not just for those already in a facility. Veterans receiving care at home from a hired caregiver or family member may qualify.
  • The net worth limit ($163,699) includes both assets and projected annual income. Primary residence and one vehicle are excluded.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Can a veteran in an assisted living facility qualify?
A: Yes. Assisted living is specifically listed as qualifying care. The facility costs count as a medical expense that can offset income for VA pension purposes.

Slide 7 of 9

2026 Aid & Attendance Rates

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Veteran alone: $2,424/month (2026)
  • Veteran with a dependent (e.g., spouse): $2,874/month (2026)
  • Surviving spouse: $1,558/month (2026)
  • Two veterans (both qualifying): $3,845/month (2026)
  • Rates are adjusted annually with COLA — verify current rates at va.gov

How to Explain It

  • Put the numbers in context: "$2,424/month can cover a significant portion of home care or assisted living costs. For many veterans, this is the difference between staying home and going to a facility."
  • Emphasize the surviving spouse benefit — $1,558/month is available to eligible widows/widowers of wartime veterans, even if the veteran never applied while alive.
  • These rates are effective December 1, 2025 (the VA's fiscal year runs December to November). Source: va.gov pension rates page.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Is the A&A benefit taxable?
A: No. VA Aid & Attendance benefits are not subject to federal income tax.
Q: How long does it take to get approved?
A: VA pension/A&A claims can take several months. Filing early and using an accredited VSO or claims agent speeds the process. Back pay is awarded to the date of the original claim.

Eligibility & the 5 ADLs

What This Slide Covers

  • The 5 Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) used by VA to assess care needs: bathing, dressing, eating, transferring (getting in/out of bed/chair), toileting
  • Difficulty with 2 or more ADLs generally qualifies a veteran for A&A consideration
  • Housebound status: a related benefit for veterans substantially confined to home who don't meet A&A thresholds

Facilitation Questions

  • Use this discussion to reinforce that A&A is not just for the most severely disabled — partial difficulty with ADLs combined with other care factors can qualify.
  • Encourage participants to share this information with parents, in-laws, and older veterans they know who may not be aware.

Managing Your Retirement Income

7 Slides — Income Stacking · Taxes · Debt · RMDs · Inflation

Slide 1 of 7 — Discussion

Income Stacking: Your Retirement Paycheck

What This Slide Covers

  • Most military retirees receive income from multiple sources simultaneously: pension, VA disability comp, Social Security, TSP/IRA distributions, and possibly part-time work or survivor benefits
  • "Income stacking" refers to how these streams combine — and how their different tax treatments affect net take-home pay
  • Understanding your total picture is the prerequisite for tax planning, debt management, and investment decisions

Facilitation Questions

Discussion Prompt: "How many income streams are you currently receiving or expecting to receive? Let's go around and count them: pension, VA comp, Social Security, TSP, spouse's income, part-time work..." This helps participants realize they may have more resources than they think — and more complexity to manage.

How to Explain It

  • Draw a simple ledger on the board: list each income stream, its approximate amount, and whether it's taxable (pension: yes; VA comp: no; SS: partially; TSP traditional: yes; Roth: no).
  • The tax-free sources (VA comp, Roth distributions) improve the real value of that income compared to what appears on paper.
  • Encourage participants to create their own version of this ledger as a household budgeting tool.

Slide 2 of 7

Federal Taxation

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Military retirement pay is fully taxable as ordinary income at the federal level
  • VA disability compensation is completely exempt from federal income tax
  • Social Security: 0–85% of benefits may be taxable depending on combined income thresholds
  • Traditional TSP/IRA distributions: fully taxable as ordinary income
  • Roth TSP/Roth IRA qualified distributions: tax-free
  • Medicare IRMAA surcharges are triggered by income from 2 years prior — large one-time withdrawals can cause unexpected Medicare cost increases

How to Explain It

  • Walk through each income source's tax treatment clearly and simply. Use the ledger from Slide 1 and add a "taxable?" column.
  • Key planning insight: retirees in lower tax brackets may benefit from Roth conversions before RMDs push them into higher brackets.
  • The IRMAA ripple effect: a large TSP withdrawal in one year raises Medicare Part B premiums 2 years later. Plan large distributions carefully.
  • This is not tax advice — recommend participants work with a CPA or tax professional for their specific situation.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Is my military pension taxed like a regular paycheck?
A: Yes — federal income tax is withheld by DFAS based on your W-4 election. You can adjust withholding at myPay.dfas.mil. State taxation varies (see Slide 3).

Slide 3 of 7 — Discussion

State Tax Landscape (2026)

What This Slide Covers

  • State tax treatment of military retirement pay varies significantly — some states fully exempt it, others tax it at ordinary income rates
  • As of 2026, over 30 states offer full or partial exemption of military retirement pay
  • States with no income tax (e.g., FL, TX, WA, NV, WY, SD, AK, TN, NH on earned income) are fully exempt by definition
  • Residency and domicile rules determine which state's taxes apply — changing domicile is a legal process, not just moving

Facilitation Questions

Discussion Prompt: "Is anyone here considering relocating in retirement — or already in a different state from where you served? State tax treatment of your pension can mean thousands of dollars a year." Use this to spark conversation about real-world tax planning.

How to Explain It

  • Encourage participants to research their specific state at militarybenefitstax.org or their state's Department of Revenue.
  • VA disability compensation is exempt in all 50 states (it's federal tax-exempt income).
  • Social Security state taxation: a majority of states do not tax SS benefits. A handful do to varying degrees.
  • The relocation decision involves many factors beyond taxes — cost of living, healthcare access, proximity to VA facilities, family. Don't recommend a move for tax reasons alone.

Slide 4 of 7

Debt in Retirement: The Reality

Key Facts & Numbers

  • More Americans are entering retirement carrying debt than in previous generations — mortgages, auto loans, and credit card balances are common
  • Carrying high-interest debt (credit cards, personal loans) on fixed income erodes purchasing power rapidly
  • Mortgage debt in retirement is not automatically bad — a low fixed-rate mortgage may be preferable to depleting savings to pay it off
  • The "debt danger zone": any debt with interest rate above your expected investment return deserves early payoff priority

How to Explain It

  • Frame this as a cash flow problem: "On a fixed income, every dollar of debt service is a dollar unavailable for living expenses, healthcare, or emergencies."
  • High-interest consumer debt is the priority target. Paying off a 20% credit card balance is a guaranteed 20% return — better than any investment.
  • For mortgages: run the math before paying off early. If the mortgage rate is 3% and your investment return is 6%, keeping the mortgage and staying invested may be better.
  • Resources: CFPB (consumerfinance.gov) has free tools for military and veteran debt management.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Should I use my TSP to pay off debt?
A: Rarely a good idea. TSP withdrawals are taxable income — a large withdrawal could bump you into a higher bracket, costing more in taxes than the debt interest. Exhaust other options first. Consult a financial advisor before tapping retirement accounts to pay debt.

Slide 5 of 7

Managing Required Minimum Distributions

Key Facts & Numbers

  • RMD age: 73 if born 1951–1959; 75 if born 1960 or later
  • Penalty for missing RMD: 25% of the amount not withdrawn (reduced to 10% if corrected in the same or following year)
  • RMD is calculated from prior December 31 account balance ÷ IRS life expectancy factor (Uniform Lifetime Table)
  • Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs): up to $105,000/year can be sent directly from IRA/TSP to a charity — counts toward RMD, but not included in taxable income

How to Explain It

  • This slide digs deeper into RMDs than Module 1. Now focus on strategies to manage the tax impact.
  • For those approaching RMD age with large balances: consider Roth conversions in lower-income years to reduce future RMD amounts.
  • QCDs are a powerful tool for charitable veterans — they satisfy the RMD, keep income off the tax return (reducing IRMAA exposure), and support causes they care about.
  • Multiple account coordination: if you have both a TSP and a traditional IRA, you must take RMDs from each — they can't be combined.

Anticipated Questions

Q: My spouse died and I inherited their IRA. Do I have RMDs on it?
A: Spouses have special rules — they can roll the inherited IRA into their own IRA and use their own RMD schedule. Non-spouse beneficiaries generally must deplete inherited IRAs within 10 years under SECURE 2.0. Recommend an estate/tax advisor for inherited account questions.

Slide 6 of 7

Inflation & Your Buying Power

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Military retirement pay and VA disability comp receive annual COLA adjustments tied to the Consumer Price Index (CPI-W)
  • Social Security also receives annual COLA tied to CPI-W
  • TSP and IRA balances do NOT automatically adjust for inflation — investment returns must outpace inflation for real purchasing power to grow
  • At 3% average annual inflation, purchasing power halves in approximately 24 years. At 4%, it halves in about 18 years.

How to Explain It

  • The good news: the indexed income sources (pension, VA comp, Social Security) are protected against inflation. This is a structural advantage most private-sector retirees don't have.
  • The risk: healthcare costs and long-term care costs inflate faster than the general CPI. Planning for healthcare cost growth specifically is important.
  • TSP allocation matters over a 20–30 year retirement: too conservative (all G Fund) means inflation slowly erodes real value. Some equity exposure (C, S, I Funds) provides growth potential.
  • This is not investment advice — but frame it as: the G Fund protects against market loss, not against inflation loss.

Anticipated Questions

Q: How has COLA kept up with inflation historically?
A: COLA has tracked CPI-W reasonably well in most years, but in high-inflation years (2021–2023) adjustments were substantial. The protection is real but imperfect since healthcare inflation often outpaces CPI. Encourage participants to review their spending each year and adjust accordingly.

Legacy & Estate Planning

9 Slides — SBP · DIC · Estate Essentials · Beneficiaries · Cognitive Decline

Slide 1 of 9

Understanding Your Survivor Benefit Plan

Key Facts & Numbers

  • SBP premium: 6.5% of the elected base amount (e.g., 6.5% of gross retirement pay if full base elected)
  • Survivor benefit: 55% of the elected base amount — paid monthly to the surviving spouse/beneficiary for life
  • SBP enrollment is a one-time election made at retirement — changing coverage requires a qualifying life event or the open season (rare)
  • SBP survivor income is taxable to the recipient
  • SBP coverage is available for spouse, child(ren), or both; former spouse coverage also available under specific conditions

How to Explain It

  • Frame SBP as insurance: "SBP is income replacement insurance for your spouse. If you die first, they continue to receive 55% of your elected retirement base — for life."
  • The premium (6.5%) is paid from retirement pay, reducing take-home pay. At retirement, this is a critical budgeting factor.
  • Without SBP: when the veteran dies, military retirement pay stops entirely. Social Security survivor benefit continues, but it's often insufficient to maintain the household's standard of living alone.
  • With SBP: the surviving spouse receives the SBP annuity + Social Security survivor benefit + any DIC (if applicable).

Anticipated Questions

Q: Can I cancel SBP if I change my mind?
A: Only during a limited window (1–3 years into retirement, with spouse's written consent) or after qualifying life events (divorce, death of beneficiary). Outside those windows, cancellation is not permitted. This makes the initial election extremely important.

Slide 2 of 9

Premiums & Paid-Up Status

Key Facts & Numbers

  • SBP is "paid up" when the veteran has paid premiums for 360 months (30 years) AND has reached age 70
  • After paid-up status: SBP coverage continues for life at no additional cost — premiums stop entirely
  • Both conditions must be met: 360 months AND age 70. Reaching age 70 early doesn't stop premiums; 360 months without age 70 doesn't stop premiums.
  • For those who retired at 20 years of service at age 38: paid-up at age 68 (360 months reached at 68, but must wait to 70)

How to Explain It

  • Walk through the timeline: "If you retired at age 42 and enrolled in SBP, you'll reach 360 months at age 72 — one condition met. But you also need to be at least 70 — already met at 72. Premiums stop at 72."
  • Paid-up status is a significant financial milestone. For veterans nearing 30 years of enrollment, point out that they're close to "free" coverage.
  • This is also a powerful argument for those hesitating: the long-term cost is finite, and coverage lasts a lifetime after that.

Slide 3 of 9 — Discussion

What If You Declined SBP?

What This Slide Covers

  • Veterans who declined SBP at retirement have very limited options to re-enroll
  • SBP open enrollment periods are rare and legislatively authorized — there is no standing open enrollment
  • Alternative strategies: term life insurance (can approximate SBP's income replacement function), permanent life insurance, or spouse's own income/savings
  • DIC (covered in Slide 4) may provide some income to surviving spouses of veterans who die from service-connected causes — but is not guaranteed and has its own eligibility criteria

Facilitation Questions

Discussion Prompt: "For those who declined SBP at retirement — what was your reasoning, and does your situation still justify that decision?" This is a sensitive topic. Keep it constructive: the goal is forward planning, not regret. Focus on what options are available now.

How to Explain It

  • If someone declined SBP and now wants coverage, their best options: purchase private life insurance (while still insurable) and structure payouts to replicate income replacement.
  • For those who declined because they didn't want to pay for years before the spouse might benefit: remind them that paid-up status at 360 months + age 70 addresses the long-cost concern.

Slide 4 of 9

Dependency & Indemnity Compensation (DIC)

Key Facts & Numbers

  • DIC base rate (2026): $1,699.36/month (effective December 1, 2025) — paid to surviving spouses of veterans who died from a service-connected condition
  • DIC is tax-free federal income
  • SBP-DIC offset fully eliminated: Since January 1, 2023, surviving spouses receive BOTH SBP and DIC in full — no longer reduced (NDAA 2020)
  • Additional allowances available: spouse with dependent child, transitional benefit (first 2 years), Aid & Attendance allowance for surviving spouse

How to Explain It

  • Clarify the SBP-DIC offset elimination — this is a major recent change many veterans and spouses are unaware of. Prior to 2023, DIC was offset dollar-for-dollar against SBP. That is no longer the case.
  • DIC is not automatic — surviving spouses must apply through VA after the veteran's death.
  • The veteran must have died from a service-connected condition, or have been 100% P&T rated for 10+ years at time of death (regardless of cause).
  • Combining SBP ($1,100–$2,000+ depending on base) + DIC ($1,699/month) + Social Security survivor benefit can provide meaningful income protection for surviving spouses.

Anticipated Questions

Q: My husband was 100% P&T for 12 years. If he dies of a heart attack (not service-connected), does DIC still apply?
A: Yes. If a veteran was continuously rated 100% P&T for 10 or more years at time of death, the surviving spouse qualifies for DIC regardless of the cause of death.

Slide 5 of 9

Estate Essentials & VA Burial

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Core estate documents every veteran should have: Will or Revocable Living Trust, Durable Power of Attorney (financial), Healthcare Power of Attorney / Advance Directive, Living Will / POLST
  • VA burial benefits (veterans with honorable discharge): burial in a national cemetery at no cost, government headstone/marker, burial flag, Presidential Memorial Certificate
  • VA burial allowance (service-connected death): up to $2,000 toward burial costs
  • VA burial allowance (non-service-connected): up to $948 if veteran was receiving VA pension or comp at time of death

How to Explain It

  • Estate documents are not just for the wealthy. Every veteran should have at minimum a will, POA, and healthcare directive.
  • National cemetery burial is a meaningful benefit — encourage veterans and families to pre-plan and notify next-of-kin of their wishes.
  • National Cemetery Scheduling Office: 1-800-535-1117 or va.gov/burials-memorials/
  • Distinguish between a will and a trust: wills go through probate (public, slow, can be contested); revocable living trusts avoid probate but cost more to set up.

Anticipated Questions

Q: My spouse is not a veteran. Can they be buried with me in a national cemetery?
A: Yes. Spouses and dependent children of eligible veterans may be buried in national cemeteries at no cost, provided space is available.

Slide 6 of 9 — Discussion

Beneficiary Designation Checklist

What This Slide Covers

  • Beneficiary designations control who receives financial accounts outside probate: TSP, IRAs, life insurance, bank POD accounts
  • Outdated designations (ex-spouses, deceased relatives) are one of the most common and preventable estate disasters
  • Beneficiary designations supersede the will — a will cannot override a beneficiary form
  • Key accounts to review: TSP, IRA/Roth IRA, life insurance (SGLI/VGLI/private), annuities, bank accounts with TOD/POD designations

Facilitation Questions

Discussion Prompt: "I want everyone in this room to commit to one action this week: log in to one account — TSP, life insurance, or your IRA — and verify the beneficiary. Just one. Who's willing to do that?" This is a concrete, high-impact action that costs nothing.

How to Explain It

  • Share real-world consequences of outdated beneficiaries: ex-spouse receives TSP instead of current spouse; children from first marriage receive life insurance instead of current dependents.
  • SGLI and VGLI beneficiary designations are separate — remind veterans who have VGLI to check their VGLI beneficiary at vgli.com.
  • Distribute the Beneficiary Designation Checklist handout and give participants 5 minutes to start filling it in during the session.

Slide 7 of 9

Protecting Your Estate

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Federal estate tax exemption (2026): $13.61 million per individual (indexed) — most veterans are below this threshold
  • State estate/inheritance taxes vary — some states have much lower exemptions (as low as $1 million)
  • Irrevocable trusts can remove assets from the taxable estate but require careful legal structuring
  • Joint tenancy with right of survivorship (JTWROS) passes assets directly to the surviving joint owner outside probate

How to Explain It

  • For most veterans, federal estate tax is not a concern. But state estate taxes and the complexity of asset transfer are real issues.
  • The primary estate protection goals for most veterans: avoiding probate delays (use beneficiary designations, trusts, or joint ownership), ensuring the right people receive assets, and protecting a surviving spouse from financial disruption.
  • Encourage veterans with complex situations (blended families, business interests, significant real estate, or disabled dependents) to consult an estate attorney.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Do I need a trust, or is a will enough?
A: For most veterans, a will + proper beneficiary designations + POD/TOD accounts handles the majority of asset transfers without probate. A trust becomes valuable when you have real estate in multiple states, a disabled beneficiary, minor children, or significant assets to protect from creditors. An estate attorney can advise.

Slide 8 of 9

Planning for Cognitive Decline

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Approximately 1 in 9 Americans over 65 has Alzheimer's disease; prevalence increases with age
  • Veterans with TBI, PTSD, or certain toxic exposures may have elevated risk for cognitive decline
  • Legal documents needed before cognitive decline: Durable Power of Attorney, Healthcare POA, Living Will/Advance Directive
  • VA fiduciary program: if a veteran is determined incompetent to manage their VA benefits, a fiduciary (family member or appointed) manages funds on their behalf

How to Explain It

  • Frame proactively: "This is not a conversation about worst-case scenarios. It's about making sure your wishes are documented and legally protected before you need that protection."
  • The most critical document: a Durable Power of Attorney. Without it, family members may need court-ordered guardianship (expensive, slow, public) to manage finances.
  • VA fiduciary program: va.gov/decision-reviews/fiduciary-program/ — designed for veterans who need help managing VA benefits.
  • Elder financial abuse: veterans with cognitive decline are prime targets. Trusted contacts on financial accounts and regular review by family members are protective.

Anticipated Questions

Q: Can my spouse automatically manage my accounts if I become incapacitated?
A: Not without legal authorization. A jointly held account can be managed by the surviving joint owner, but individual accounts (TSP, IRA, investment accounts) require a Durable POA or court-ordered guardianship. Don't assume spousal access — plan it legally in advance.
Q: What's the difference between a regular POA and a Durable POA?
A: A standard POA expires if the grantor becomes incapacitated. A Durable POA stays in effect even after incapacity — which is exactly when you need it most. Always use a Durable POA for estate planning purposes.
