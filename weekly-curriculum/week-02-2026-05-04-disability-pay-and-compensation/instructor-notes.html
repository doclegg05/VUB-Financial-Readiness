Slide 1

How Your Pension Works

What's on Screen

The slide shows the High-3 and BRS pension formulas side by side, with a worked example. Most veterans in the room will be High-3 retirees.

Key Numbers

  • High-3 formula: Average of highest 36 months of basic pay × 2.5% × years of service
  • BRS formula: High-3 average × 2.0% × years of service (plus government TSP match during service)
  • Example (20 years, High-3): $7,000/mo average → $3,500/month pension (50%)
  • Maximum pension cap: 75% of pay base
  • COLA: Adjusts annually with CPI-W (same index as Social Security)
  • Final Pay system: For service members who entered before September 8, 1980 — uses final basic pay, not a 36-month average

How to Say It

  • Open simply: "Your military pension is one of the most valuable financial assets you have. It's a guaranteed monthly payment for life, adjusted for inflation, and it can't go bankrupt. Very few private-sector workers get anything like it."
  • "Under High-3, you earn 2.5% of your pay for each year you served. Twenty years equals 50%. Twenty-four years equals 60%. The number on your check is 2.5 times your years, applied to your highest average 36-month pay."
  • "If anyone in the room is under BRS — that's the Blended Retirement System for those who entered after January 2018 — the multiplier is 2% instead of 2.5%, but you got government TSP contributions during your service to make up the difference."
  • Emphasize COLA: "One thing that makes this pension truly powerful is the inflation adjustment. Your pension check went up just like Social Security did — it's not a fixed number that slowly loses value. In 2022, both went up over 8%."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "I retired under Final Pay — how is that different?"

A: Final Pay applies if you entered service before September 8, 1980. Instead of averaging your highest 36 months, it uses your actual final basic pay as the base. The 2.5% multiplier works the same way. Your base is likely slightly higher than a High-3 average since it uses your top pay, not an average.

Q: "Can my pension be reduced or taken away?"

A: Generally no. Your pension is protected. It can only be reduced through a legal action such as a court-ordered division in a divorce (under USFSPA), or if you are recalled to active duty and your pay is adjusted accordingly. The federal government has never defaulted on military retirement pay.

Q: "Does my pension count as income for taxes?"

A: Yes, military retirement pay is taxed as ordinary income at the federal level. West Virginia state taxation depends on current state law — we'll cover that in Module 4. You receive a 1099-R from DFAS each January.

Slide 2

Reading Your Pension Statement

What's on Screen

An annotated sample Retiree Account Statement (RAS) from myPay/DFAS, with callouts for gross pay, deductions (SBP, TRICARE, taxes), and net pay.

Key Numbers

  • Gross retired pay: What DFAS calculates based on your formula before any deductions
  • VA waiver: If you also receive VA disability compensation, a corresponding offset may appear here — this is key territory for Module 2
  • SBP premium: If enrolled, 6.5% of your selected base amount is deducted here
  • TRICARE/FEDVIP premiums: May appear as deductions if enrolled via DFAS
  • Federal withholding: Based on your W-4P on file with DFAS

How to Say It

  • "If you've never really looked at your Retiree Account Statement, let's change that today. Log into myPay.dfas.mil and pull it up. Every number on this document has a story."
  • "The most important thing to check is the VA offset line, if you have one. Some veterans don't realize how much of their retirement pay is being offset by their VA compensation — and they may qualify for CRDP or CRSC to change that. We'll dig into that in Module 2."
  • "Also check your federal tax withholding. Many retirees are underwithholding because their income has changed — especially if they started Social Security or are drawing from TSP. Getting surprised by a big tax bill is avoidable."
  • Encourage action: "If anything on your statement doesn't make sense, DFAS has a customer service line: 1-800-321-1080. Don't just assume it's right."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "How do I get my 1099-R?"

A: It's available on myPay at myPay.dfas.mil each January. You can also call DFAS at 1-800-321-1080 to have a paper copy mailed. If you have both retirement pay and VA compensation, be aware that the 1099-R only covers taxable retired pay — VA disability compensation does not appear on a 1099-R because it is not taxable.

Q: "What if I think my retirement pay amount is wrong?"

A: Contact DFAS directly. Keep records of what you think it should be and what you're receiving. For complex situations — especially anything involving the VA offset or CRDP — a VSO can help you navigate the paperwork.

Q: "Can I change my tax withholding?"

A: Yes. Log into myPay and update your W-4P at any time. If you owe a significant amount at tax time, consider increasing withholding — especially once you add Social Security and TSP withdrawals to your income picture.

Slide 3

VA Disability Compensation

What's on Screen

Overview of VA disability compensation: how ratings work, how pay scales with rating percentage, and the tax-free nature of VA comp payments.

Key Numbers

  • Tax status: VA disability compensation is 100% federal tax-free (and exempt from state income taxes in virtually all states)
  • Rating scale: 0%, 10%, 20%, 30%... up to 100% (plus 100% P&T — Permanent and Total)
  • Combined ratings: VA uses a "whole person" method, not simple addition — the math can seem counterintuitive
  • COLA: VA compensation rates are adjusted each December 1st based on Social Security COLA
  • SMC (Special Monthly Compensation): Available for veterans with specific severe disabilities (loss of use of limb, need for aid and attendance, etc.) — can significantly exceed the standard 100% rate

How to Say It

  • "VA disability compensation is money you earned by serving. It is not taxed, it doesn't count as income for most means-tested programs, and it adjusts with inflation every December."
  • "The rating system is designed to reflect how much your service-connected conditions affect your ability to work and your daily life. A 30% rating doesn't mean you're 30% disabled in a literal sense — it's more of a benefit scale."
  • "The combined rating calculation is confusing. Two 50% ratings don't equal 100%. The VA starts with your whole person — 100% — and applies each rating successively to the remainder. A VSO can help you understand your combined rating."
  • "If you have severe disabilities — loss of a limb, need for round-the-clock care — ask your VSO specifically about SMC. Those rates can be substantially higher than standard 100%."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Does VA comp affect my retirement pay?"

A: If you have both, the VA offset (also called the VA waiver) historically reduced retirement pay dollar-for-dollar by the amount of VA comp. But if you meet the rating threshold for CRDP — 50% or higher combined rating — you can receive both without offset. We go into all of this detail in Module 2.

Q: "Can I still file a claim if I've been out for 20 years?"

A: Yes. There is no time limit on filing an initial claim for VA disability compensation. You can file at any age, any time after separation. The rating is assigned based on your current condition and its nexus to your service, not how long ago you served. Many veterans file or refile for the first time in their 60s and 70s.

Q: "What's P&T status and why does it matter?"

A: P&T stands for Permanent and Total — it means the VA has determined your disability is not expected to improve and you are rated 100%. P&T status unlocks additional benefits: CHAMPVA for dependents, Commissary and Exchange access, property tax exemptions in many states, and no future re-examinations. It's a meaningful status to pursue if you believe your conditions warrant it.

Slide 4

Your Retirement Savings Account

What's on Screen

Overview of the Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) — the five core funds (G, F, C, S, I) and the Lifecycle (L) funds — plus tax treatment differences between Traditional and Roth TSP.

Key Numbers

  • G Fund: Government Securities — no credit risk, low return (historically ~4–5%), guaranteed not to lose principal
  • C Fund: S&P 500 index — moderate-to-high risk, historically ~10% annual average over long periods
  • S Fund: Small-cap stocks (companies not in S&P 500) — higher risk/reward than C Fund
  • I Fund: International developed markets — adds geographic diversification
  • F Fund: Bond index — lower risk than stock funds, interest rate sensitive
  • L Funds: Target-date lifecycle funds that shift automatically from aggressive to conservative as target date approaches
  • Traditional TSP: Contributions pre-tax, withdrawals taxed as ordinary income
  • Roth TSP: Contributions post-tax, qualified withdrawals tax-free

How to Say It

  • "Think of the TSP like a retirement cafeteria with five main options. G is the safest — you can't lose what you put in. C tracks America's 500 biggest companies. S and I add more variety. F is bonds. The L Funds are the auto-pilot version: pick your retirement decade and let it manage itself."
  • "If you were in the military and you contributed to TSP, you very likely still have that account open. Log into TSP.gov or call 1-877-968-3778 if you've lost access. Even a $10,000 balance left untouched for 10–15 years makes a difference."
  • "One thing many veterans don't know: the TSP has some of the lowest fees in the investment world — around 0.04% per year. That means more of your money stays working for you compared to most private IRAs or 401(k)s."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Should I roll my TSP into an IRA?"

A: This is a personal financial decision, and I can't advise you on what's right for your situation. What I can tell you is: TSP has exceptionally low fees, which is a real advantage. IRAs offer more investment options. And if you're between age 55 and 59½ and separated from service at 55 or older, rolling to an IRA removes your penalty-free withdrawal access. Consult a financial advisor before making this move.

Q: "What if I'm 100% in G Fund — is that bad?"

A: It depends on your full financial picture, time horizon, and other income sources. G Fund has never lost principal, which some people need for peace of mind. The concern is that over a 20–30 year retirement, G Fund returns may not keep pace with inflation. This is a conversation to have with a financial advisor who knows your complete situation.

Q: "I forgot about my TSP years ago. Can I get it back?"

A: Very likely, yes. If your balance was over $200 at separation, your account stays open. Log into TSP.gov or call the ThriftLine at 1-877-968-3778. You'll need your TSP account number or Social Security number to verify your identity. DFAS can also help locate it if needed.

Slide 5

Knowing Your Plan

What's on Screen

A visual framework for mapping all income sources — pension, VA comp, TSP/IRA, Social Security, part-time work — and the concept of sequencing withdrawals. Encourages veterans to inventory what they have before making withdrawal decisions.

Key Numbers

  • Most veterans in this room have 3–5 potential income sources in retirement
  • The sequencing of which accounts to draw from — and when — meaningfully affects lifetime taxes and benefits
  • Required Minimum Distributions start at age 73 (born 1951–1959) or 75 (born 1960+), forcing TSP and IRA withdrawals regardless of whether you need the income

How to Say It

  • "Before you make any withdrawal decision or tax strategy, you need to know what you're working with. Most of you have more sources of retirement income than a typical American — pension, possibly VA comp, TSP, maybe Social Security, maybe a spouse's income. That's good news, but it also means more moving parts."
  • "This slide is about taking inventory. The exercise isn't to make any decisions today — it's to write down every income source you have or expect to have, and what the tax character of each one is. That's the foundation of any real retirement plan."
  • "Tax character matters a lot. VA comp: not taxed. Pension: taxed as income. TSP Traditional: taxed as income when withdrawn. Roth TSP: tax-free when withdrawn. Social Security: partially taxed. Knowing which buckets you're drawing from changes your tax picture significantly."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "What order should I draw from my accounts in?"

A: The optimal sequence depends on your tax bracket, expected future income, account sizes, and how long you plan to keep assets working. There are common strategies — taxable first, tax-deferred second, Roth last — but I'm not in a position to tell you the right answer for your situation. A fee-only financial planner or a CERTIFIED FINANCIAL PLANNER™ professional can model this out for you.

Q: "I haven't done any real financial planning. Is it too late?"

A: It is never too late to understand what you have and make informed decisions going forward. Even someone in their 70s can benefit from understanding how RMDs interact with taxes, or whether their estate plan reflects their wishes. Start with the inventory exercise on this slide.

Slide 6

Withdrawals & Required Minimum Distributions

What's on Screen

TSP and IRA withdrawal options side by side, with RMD ages, penalty rules, and the 10% early-withdrawal exception for military retirees (the "Rule of 55").

Key Numbers

  • RMD age — born 1951–1959: Age 73 (SECURE 2.0 Act)
  • RMD age — born 1960 or later: Age 75
  • Penalty for missing RMD: 25% of the amount that should have been withdrawn (reduced to 10% if corrected within 2 years)
  • Early withdrawal penalty: 10% on withdrawals before age 59½
  • Rule of 55 exception: If you separated from federal service in the calendar year you turned 55 or later, TSP withdrawals are penalty-free — but this does NOT transfer to an IRA
  • Roth TSP / Roth IRA: No RMDs for Roth IRA; Roth TSP must be rolled to Roth IRA to avoid RMDs. Qualified withdrawals tax-free.

How to Say It

  • "The IRS requires you to start withdrawing from traditional tax-deferred accounts — TSP, IRAs — once you hit a certain age. This is the Required Minimum Distribution. Think of it as the government saying, 'You've been deferring taxes long enough. Time to pay up.'"
  • "For most of you, the RMD age is 73. If you were born in 1960 or later, it's 75. Miss an RMD and you owe a 25% penalty on whatever you should have taken. That's on top of the regular income tax. It's a significant penalty worth avoiding."
  • "Here's something many veterans don't know: if you left federal service the same year you turned 55 or older, you can take TSP withdrawals without the 10% early penalty — even if you're under 59½. This is only for TSP, not IRAs. Rolling your TSP into an IRA removes that advantage."
  • "Roth accounts work differently. Roth IRA has no RMDs during your lifetime. Roth TSP technically does — but you can roll it to a Roth IRA to avoid that."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "How do I calculate my RMD?"

A: Your RMD is calculated by dividing your prior year-end account balance by an IRS life expectancy factor from the Uniform Lifetime Table. TSP.gov and IRS Publication 590-B both have the tables and calculators. Your account custodian (TSP, IRA broker) will usually calculate and notify you of your RMD amount each year.

Q: "Can I withdraw more than the RMD?"

A: Yes. The RMD is a minimum, not a maximum. You can always withdraw more — you'll just owe income tax on whatever you take out from a traditional (pre-tax) account. Some people strategically withdraw more in lower-income years to reduce future RMDs.

Q: "What if I don't need the money? Can I just give the RMD to charity?"

A: Yes — there is a provision called a Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD) that lets IRA owners age 70½ or older transfer up to $105,000 per year directly to a qualifying charity. This counts toward your RMD and is excluded from your taxable income. It's a powerful strategy for people who don't need the income. Consult a tax advisor about whether this applies to your TSP versus IRA situation.

Slide 7

Social Security for Veterans

What's on Screen

Social Security basics for veterans: how military service credits work, earnings history, and when benefits can begin (62 through 70).

Key Numbers

  • Earliest claiming age: 62
  • Full Retirement Age (FRA) for born 1960 or later: 67
  • FRA for born 1955–1959: Sliding scale from 66 years 2 months (born 1955) to 66 years 10 months (born 1959)
  • Latest claiming age for delayed credits: 70
  • Delayed credits past FRA: 8% per year (or approximately 0.67% per month)
  • Military service earnings credits: Active-duty service members receive Social Security earnings credits for their military pay — military service does count toward your Social Security record
  • SSA earnings statement: Available at ssa.gov/myaccount

How to Say It

  • "Your military service absolutely counts toward Social Security. Every year of active duty, every dollar of military pay — it's all on your earnings record. You can see your full record at SSA.gov, and you should check it for accuracy every few years."
  • "Social Security is not a pension — it's an earned benefit calculated on your lifetime earnings. The more you earned (up to the taxable wage base), and the longer you worked, the higher your benefit."
  • "You can start receiving Social Security as early as 62, but that locks in a permanently reduced amount. Wait until your Full Retirement Age — which for most of you is in the 66-to-67 range — and you get your full 'Primary Insurance Amount,' or PIA. Wait until 70 and it grows 8% per year past FRA."
  • "I cannot tell you when to claim. The right answer depends on your health, your spouse's situation, your other income, and your long-term tax picture. What I can show you is the math — and we'll do that on Slide 9."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Does my VA disability comp reduce my Social Security?"

A: No. VA disability compensation has no impact on Social Security benefits. They are completely separate programs. You can receive full Social Security and full VA comp simultaneously with no offset.

Q: "I never worked a civilian job. Can I still get Social Security?"

A: Yes, if your active duty military pay was covered by Social Security (which it has been since 1957 for regular active duty and since 1988 for Guard/Reserve drill pay). Every year of military service counts. Log into SSA.gov to see your earnings history and estimated benefit.

Q: "What happens to my Social Security if I keep working?"

A: If you are under your FRA and receiving Social Security, there is an earnings limit — earning above it reduces your benefit temporarily (you get it back after FRA). Once you reach FRA, you can earn any amount and your Social Security is not reduced. This is an important planning point for anyone thinking about part-time work in early retirement.

Slide 8

Spousal & Survivor Benefits

What's on Screen

Overview of Social Security spousal benefits (up to 50% of primary earner's PIA) and survivor benefits (up to 100% of the deceased's benefit), with key ages and rules for each.

Key Numbers

  • Spousal benefit maximum: 50% of the primary earner's PIA, if claimed at the spouse's own FRA
  • Spousal benefit earliest claiming age: 62 (with reduction)
  • Survivor benefit maximum: 100% of the deceased spouse's benefit (if survivor claims at their own FRA or later)
  • Survivor benefit earliest claiming age: 60 (50 if disabled); 100% if waiting until own FRA or deceased's age-70 benefit
  • Government Pension Offset (GPO): Reduces Social Security spousal/survivor benefits if the spouse receives a non-covered government pension — may affect some WV public employees. WEP (Windfall Elimination Provision) was largely repealed for most workers under Social Security Fairness Act 2024.

How to Say It

  • "For married veterans, Social Security is not just your benefit — it's potentially two benefits. Your spouse may be entitled to up to 50% of your PIA even if they have little or no earnings history of their own."
  • "If you pass away before your spouse, they receive a survivor benefit — up to 100% of what you were receiving. This is why the decision about when to claim Social Security is not just about you. Delaying to 70 can lock in a much larger survivor benefit for your spouse for decades after you're gone."
  • "There's a provision called the Government Pension Offset. If your spouse receives a pension from a government job that didn't pay into Social Security — some state or local government jobs — their Social Security spousal or survivor benefit can be reduced. This is worth checking if your spouse worked in state or local government."
  • North Star reminder: "Again — when to claim is a personal decision I can't make for you. What I want you to leave knowing is that these options exist and your spouse's situation is part of the calculation."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Can my spouse get Social Security from both their own work history and my record?"

A: They can receive the higher of the two — their own earned benefit or the spousal benefit. SSA pays their own benefit first; if the spousal benefit is higher, they get the difference as a top-up. They cannot stack both amounts on top of each other.

Q: "If I'm divorced, does my ex-spouse get my Social Security?"

A: An ex-spouse may be eligible for spousal benefits if the marriage lasted at least 10 years, they are currently unmarried, and they are age 62 or older. Crucially, this does not reduce your own benefit at all — what SSA pays your ex-spouse comes from the SSA's own account, not yours.

Q: "What is WEP and does it affect me?"

A: The Windfall Elimination Provision used to reduce Social Security benefits for people who also received a pension from non-covered employment. The Social Security Fairness Act, signed in January 2025, largely eliminated WEP for most affected workers, potentially increasing their Social Security benefits. If WEP previously applied to you or your spouse, it's worth checking with SSA about any adjustment.

Slide 9

Social Security: The Dollar Math

What's on Screen

A comparison table showing how benefit amount changes based on claiming age relative to FRA (67), with approximate multipliers and a breakeven analysis illustration.

Key Numbers

  • Claim at 62 (FRA = 67): Approximately 70% of PIA — a permanent 30% reduction
  • Claim at FRA (67): 100% of PIA
  • Claim at 70: Approximately 124% of PIA — 8% per year × 3 years past FRA
  • Delayed credit rate: 8% per year (2/3% per month) past FRA, up to age 70
  • Breakeven concept: Approximately age 80–83 to break even on waiting from 62 to 70, depending on PIA and other assumptions
  • Inflation adjustment: COLA applies to whatever benefit you're receiving — so a higher starting amount compounds to an even larger long-term advantage

How to Say It

  • "Let's look at the actual numbers. If your full retirement benefit — your PIA — is $2,000 per month, claiming at 62 gives you about $1,400. Waiting until 67 gives you the full $2,000. Waiting until 70 gives you about $2,480. Those differences compound every year with COLA adjustments."
  • "There's a crossover point — a breakeven age — where the higher monthly payments from waiting overtake the total cumulative amount you would have collected by claiming early. That crossover is typically in the early-to-mid 80s."
  • "If you're in good health and have other income to cover expenses, delaying often makes mathematical sense. If you're in poor health, or you need the income now, claiming earlier may be right for you. And if you have a younger or healthier spouse, your decision about when to claim could affect their income for many years after you're gone."
  • North Star reminder — say this explicitly: "I want to be very clear: I am not telling you when to claim. This is one of the most important financial decisions you'll make, and it has no one right answer. Use this math as a framework, and then talk to SSA or a financial advisor who can run your specific numbers."
⚠ Liability Watch-Out — SS Claiming Advice This is the slide most likely to prompt veterans to ask you directly, "Should I claim now?" Do not answer that question with a recommendation. Always redirect: "That depends on your health, your spouse, your other income, and your tax situation. Those are things a financial advisor or SSA counselor can help you model." Giving a direct recommendation — even a well-meaning one — creates liability and may be incorrect for their specific situation.

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "If I claim at 62 while still collecting my pension and VA comp, will it reduce anything?"

A: Social Security at any age does not reduce your military pension or VA disability compensation. However, if you are under FRA and have earned income from working, the Social Security earnings test may temporarily reduce your SS benefit. Your pension and VA comp are completely unaffected.

Q: "What if Social Security goes bankrupt before I claim?"

A: Current projections from the Social Security Trustees Report show the combined trust funds sufficient through the mid-2030s if no changes are made. Even in a worst case, current law allows benefits of approximately 80% of scheduled amounts. The program has been adjusted many times over its history and has broad political support. Complete elimination is not a realistic projection based on current law.

Key Takeaways — Module 1

What to Emphasize

  • Veterans in this room have more financial assets than the typical American retiree — pension, VA comp, TSP, and Social Security are all real wealth that needs to be understood and coordinated.
  • The pension formula (High-3 × 2.5% × years) is the foundation — know your numbers.
  • VA comp is tax-free; pension and TSP withdrawals are taxed — understanding the difference changes your planning.
  • RMDs start at 73 (born 1951–1959) or 75 (born 1960+) — failing to take them costs 25% of the missed amount.
  • Social Security claiming age is a major, permanent decision — from 70% of PIA at 62 to 124% at 70. Do the math before deciding.

Transition to Module 2

"Now that we've mapped what you have, let's talk about how to maximize the disability benefits side — because many veterans are leaving money on the table with their VA rating or CRDP/CRSC status."

Break Guidance

This is a good moment for a 10-minute break if you've run the full module without stopping. Tell students: "Before we come back, think about this: Do you know your current VA disability rating? Do you know if you're receiving CRDP? Write those down — they'll matter in Module 2."

Module 2

Maximizing Your Disability Benefits

8 Slides — Approx. 40–50 minutes

Slide 1

Filing New Claims & Rating Increases

What's on Screen

Overview of how to file an original claim, supplemental claim, and a rating increase request — including the Fully Developed Claim process and what triggers a rating change.

Key Numbers

  • No statute of limitations: Veterans can file initial claims or supplemental claims at any time after separation
  • Supplemental claim: Used when you have new and relevant evidence for a previously denied or under-rated condition
  • Higher-Level Review (HLR): Requests a senior reviewer to re-examine the same evidence; no new evidence needed
  • Average VA claim processing time: Varies widely; fully developed claims (all evidence submitted upfront) process faster
  • Effective date: Generally the date VA receives your claim, or the day after your prior claim was decided — timing matters for back pay

How to Say It

  • "If you are not yet receiving VA disability compensation, or if your rating hasn't been reviewed in years, today's session should motivate you to take a fresh look. The process has improved significantly with the introduction of the Appeals Modernization Act."
  • "There are three main lanes when you disagree with a VA decision: file a Supplemental Claim with new evidence, request a Higher-Level Review, or appeal to the Board of Veterans' Appeals. Each has different timelines and evidence rules."
  • "The most important concept here is the effective date. Your back pay generally starts from the date the VA receives your claim. That means filing sooner — even before you have all your evidence together — establishes an earlier effective date. You can submit additional evidence later."
  • "Use a VSO. They are accredited by the VA, they do this for free, and they know how to properly document and submit claims. A well-prepared claim moves faster and gets better outcomes."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "What if my claim was denied years ago? Can I still fight it?"

A: Yes. If you have new and relevant evidence, you can file a Supplemental Claim at any time. If you believe the prior decision was wrong based on the same evidence, a Higher-Level Review is available within one year of the decision. If those paths don't succeed, a Board of Veterans' Appeals appeal is available. A VSO can review your prior denial and recommend the best lane.

Q: "What counts as 'new evidence'?"

A: New evidence means evidence that wasn't previously considered. It must also be relevant — meaning it tends to prove or disprove something material to your claim. Private medical opinions, buddy statements from fellow service members, service records the VA didn't have, and new medical diagnoses all can qualify. The more specific the nexus between your condition and your service, the stronger the evidence.

Q: "Can I get a rating increase for a condition that's gotten worse?"

A: Absolutely. If a service-connected condition has worsened, you can file for a rating increase. You'll need current medical evidence showing the current severity. VA exam documentation from recent VA health appointments can support this.

Secondary Service-Connected Conditions

What's on Screen

Explains "secondary service connection" — when a service-connected condition causes or aggravates another condition, the secondary condition can also be rated by the VA. Common examples are shown.

Key Numbers

  • Secondary conditions: Fully ratable, just like primary service-connected conditions
  • Nexus requirement: Must show the secondary condition was caused or made worse by the primary service-connected condition
  • Common examples: PTSD → sleep apnea; knee injury → hip/back problems due to gait changes; diabetes (service-connected) → peripheral neuropathy, heart disease, erectile dysfunction, kidney disease

How to Say It

  • "Here's one of the most underutilized concepts in VA benefits: secondary service connection. If one of your service-connected conditions caused or worsened another condition, that second condition can also be rated and compensated — even if it has nothing to do with your service directly."
  • "Classic example: a veteran has a service-connected knee injury that changed the way he walks. Over the years, that changed gait caused hip problems and a back problem. Those hip and back conditions can be secondarily service-connected to the knee. Another veteran has service-connected diabetes and develops peripheral neuropathy as a complication. That neuropathy is separately ratable."
  • "PTSD and sleep apnea is currently one of the most common secondary claims being filed. VA has recognized the research connection between PTSD and sleep apnea. If you have a PTSD rating and a sleep apnea diagnosis, talk to your VSO about a secondary claim."
  • "The key is documentation: your doctor needs to state explicitly that condition B was caused by or aggravated by condition A. A letter saying 'it's possible' is weak; a letter saying 'it is at least as likely as not that...' is the legal standard the VA uses."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "How many secondary conditions can I claim?"

A: There is no limit. As long as each condition has a nexus to a service-connected condition, they can all be claimed and rated separately. VA will use the combined ratings calculation to determine your overall rating.

Q: "My VA doctor won't write the nexus letter. What do I do?"

A: You can obtain a private medical opinion from any licensed physician. Many veterans use private doctors specifically for nexus letters. A VSO can refer you to veteran-friendly medical professionals who understand what the VA needs. The letter must include the magic phrase: "it is at least as likely as not" that your condition was caused by or aggravated by your service-connected condition.

The VA Waiver (Offset)

What's on Screen

Explanation of the concurrent receipt rules: how VA disability comp historically offset military retirement pay dollar-for-dollar, and the two programs — CRDP and CRSC — that allow some veterans to receive both in full.

Key Numbers

  • VA waiver (offset): When a retiree has both military retirement pay and VA disability comp, retirement pay was historically reduced by the amount of VA comp (it was treated as a waiver of taxable retirement pay in favor of tax-free VA comp)
  • CRDP (Concurrent Retirement and Disability Pay): Eliminates the offset for retirees with a combined VA rating of 50% or higher — both payments in full
  • CRSC (Combat-Related Special Compensation): Alternative program for retirees whose disability is combat-related; requires minimum 10% VA rating; no minimum years of service requirement; does not require 50% rating threshold
  • Chapter 61 retirees: Medically retired with less than 20 years — special rules apply; CRDP not available, CRSC may be available

How to Say It

  • "For years, veterans who were both medically rated and military retirees couldn't get the full benefit of both. Your retirement pay was reduced by exactly the amount of your VA comp. You got the same total — but the portion shifted from taxable retirement pay to tax-free VA comp."
  • "Congress fixed much of this. CRDP allows retirees with 50% or higher VA rating to receive both their full retirement pay and full VA compensation. No offset. This is automatic for eligible veterans — but you need to know if you qualify."
  • "CRSC is a different path — specifically for disabilities that are directly connected to combat or certain hazardous duty. The minimum rating threshold is lower (10%), but the disability must be demonstrably combat-related. CRSC is a separate application; it isn't automatic."
  • Open the discussion: "Let's talk about this in the room — does anyone know which of these programs applies to them? And does anyone have questions about the offset that's showing up on their retirement statement?"
⚠ Liability Watch-Out — CRDP vs. CRSC Election Veterans who qualify for both CRDP and CRSC must make an annual election of which to receive. Do NOT tell a veteran which to choose. The math depends on their specific retirement pay base, VA rating, and whether their disabilities are combat-related. Calculating this incorrectly costs money. Always direct them to their VSO, a DFAS counselor, or a military financial advisor.

Facilitation Tips

Opening prompt: "How many of you are currently receiving VA disability compensation? [hands up] How many of you know whether your retirement pay is being offset? [hands up] How many of you know what CRDP or CRSC is?" This sequence usually reveals significant knowledge gaps and genuine engagement.
What good discussion looks like: Veterans sharing confusion about their own statements, asking about whether they qualify. Validate confusion — this is genuinely complicated. Your job is to explain the concepts clearly, not resolve their individual situations in the room.
Redirect: If someone asks whether they specifically should apply for CRSC or rely on CRDP: "That's exactly the right question to bring to your VSO or a military financial advisor. The answer is different for every veteran based on your rating, your retirement pay amount, and whether your disabilities are combat-related. The handout in your packet — the CRDP/CRSC Comparison Sheet — walks through what you need to bring to that conversation."
Time: 7–10 minutes. This topic generates high engagement. Watch your clock.

CRDP vs. CRSC: Side by Side

What's on Screen

A direct comparison table of CRDP and CRSC across key dimensions: eligibility criteria, minimum rating, how the benefit is calculated, tax treatment, and who processes it.

Key Numbers

  • CRDP minimum VA rating: 50% combined rating
  • CRSC minimum VA rating: 10% (must be combat-related)
  • CRDP tax treatment: Same as regular retirement pay — taxable as ordinary income
  • CRSC tax treatment: Tax-free (like VA disability compensation)
  • CRDP administration: Automatic; processed by DFAS if you meet the 50% threshold
  • CRSC administration: Application required; submitted through your military branch service office
  • If you qualify for both: Must make an annual election (January each year); whichever you don't elect is forfeited for that year
  • Chapter 61 retirees: Not eligible for CRDP; may be eligible for CRSC

How to Say It

  • "The table on screen puts these side by side. The key difference beyond the rating thresholds is taxes: CRDP is taxable income, CRSC is tax-free. So depending on your total income and tax bracket, CRSC might produce more after-tax money even if the gross dollar amount is similar."
  • "CRDP is automatic — if your VA rating hits 50% or higher, DFAS adjusts your payment without you doing anything. CRSC requires you to apply to your branch. If you have combat-related disabilities and haven't applied for CRSC, you could be leaving tax-free money on the table."
  • "The annual election is the part that trips people up. If you qualify for both, you choose one each January. Most people pick whichever pays more after tax in their situation. Some people switch each year depending on their income. A VSO or financial advisor can help you model which is better for you."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "What qualifies as 'combat-related' for CRSC?"

A: Combat-related disabilities include those resulting from direct combat, hazardous service (combat operations, diving, parachuting, etc.), instrumentalities of war (injuries from military equipment or vehicles), or simulated war (training exercises). It does not require the veteran to have been in a war zone — a training accident with military equipment can qualify. The standard is whether the disability arose from the performance of hazardous military duty.

Q: "If I'm currently getting CRDP, do I need to apply for CRSC separately?"

A: Yes. CRSC is a separate application through your service branch. Being enrolled in CRDP doesn't automatically evaluate you for CRSC. If you have combat-related disabilities with a 10% or higher rating, it's worth having a VSO evaluate whether a CRSC application makes sense, even if CRDP is currently paying you more gross.

Covers the annual CRDP/CRSC election window, common DFAS processing delays when VA ratings change, and what to do when retirement pay doesn't adjust correctly after a rating change.

VA Pension & the 3-Year Look-Back

What's on Screen

Overview of the VA Pension (not to be confused with military retirement pension) as a means-tested benefit for low-income wartime veterans, including the net worth limit and the 3-year look-back for asset transfers.

Key Numbers

  • Program name clarity: "VA Pension" is a needs-based benefit for low-income wartime veterans — completely separate from military retirement pay
  • 2026 net worth limit: $163,699 (includes assets plus annual income)
  • Look-back period: 3 years prior to application
  • Penalty for asset transfers: If you transferred assets for less than fair market value within the 3 years before applying, a penalty period is imposed — you are ineligible for VA Pension benefits for a period proportional to the value transferred
  • Primary benefit relevance: VA Pension is most relevant as a gateway to Aid & Attendance benefits (covered in Module 3) for low-income veterans requiring care
  • Excluded assets: Primary residence, one vehicle, and certain personal property are not counted against the net worth limit

How to Say It

  • "The term 'VA Pension' is confusing because most of you are already receiving a military retirement — and you think of that as your pension. The VA Pension is something entirely different. It's a needs-based benefit for low-income veterans who served during wartime. Most veterans in this room probably don't need it — but understanding it matters because it's the doorway to Aid and Attendance, which we'll cover in Module 3."
  • "The 3-year look-back is something families trip over constantly. If a veteran or their family gives away assets — transfers the house to the kids, gifts money — within 3 years of applying for VA Pension, the VA will impose a penalty period of ineligibility. It works similarly to Medicaid's look-back rule."
  • "The net worth limit for 2026 is $163,699. Net worth includes both assets and annual income. The primary home and one vehicle are excluded. If a veteran's assets are over the limit, they need to be under that threshold — through legitimate spend-down — before they apply."
  • Important: "I cannot advise anyone to structure their finances a certain way to qualify for this benefit. That kind of planning requires an accredited VA attorney or VA-accredited financial planner. The 3-year look-back exists specifically because Congress wanted to prevent asset manipulation."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Can I get VA Pension even if I have military retirement pay?"

A: Possibly, but it's unlikely unless your net worth is very low. Military retirement pay counts as annual income toward the net worth calculation. If your combined income and assets exceed $163,699, you don't qualify. At that point, Aid & Attendance may still be accessible if your income is low enough — the rates are added to the base pension rate.

Q: "What happens to the penalty period if the transfer was to help a disabled child?"

A: There are some exceptions to the look-back penalty, including transfers to a child who is permanently and totally disabled, and transfers to certain irrevocable trusts for disabled individuals. This is a complex legal area — the veteran should consult a VA-accredited attorney before making any such transfers.

  • "These numbers aren't scare tactics — they're real. Veterans and retirees lost $419 million to fraud in 2024 alone. Military-connected consumers as a whole lost $584 million. You are a specific, targeted population because scammers know you have reliable, predictable income: pension, VA comp, maybe Social Security."
  • "The most common scam in the veteran space right now is the 'claims agent' scam. Someone tells you they can get your VA rating increased — for a fee. This is illegal. VA claims assistance is always free through an accredited VSO. If anyone is charging you money to file a VA claim, walk away."
  • "A related scam is 'pension poaching' — someone convinces veterans to transfer their assets into an annuity or trust to 'qualify for VA benefits.' Anticipated Q&A

Q: "How do I know if a VSO is legitimate?"

A: Accredited VSOs are recognized by the VA. You can search the VA's list of accredited representatives at va.gov. Major national VSOs — DAV, VFW, American Legion, AMVETS, Disabled American Veterans — are all accredited. They charge zero fees for claims assistance. The VA also has its own Veterans Benefits Administration representatives at VA offices.

Q: "What do I do if I've already been scammed?"

A: Report it immediately to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov, to your state attorney general's office, and to the VA's Office of Inspector General hotline at 1-800-488-8244 if it involves someone impersonating VA services or selling unaccredited VA claims services. Contact your bank immediately if financial information was compromised. Time matters — the sooner you report, the better the chance of recovery.

Q: "Are those 'claim maximizer' companies that advertise on TV legitimate?"

A: Many are not — or at minimum, they charge for services you can get free from an accredited VSO. Some are outright scams. Before engaging any company that charges money for VA claims assistance, verify their VA accreditation at va.gov and check their record with the Better Business Bureau and your state attorney general's office.

CHAMPVA: Coverage for Dependents

What's on Screen

Overview of CHAMPVA — the Civilian Health and Medical Program of the Department of Veterans Affairs — including who qualifies, what it covers, and the cost structure.

Key Numbers

  • Coverage rate: 75% of covered services after deductible
  • Annual deductible: $50 per person
  • Annual deductible family maximum: $100 per family
  • Annual out-of-pocket cap: $3,000 per beneficiary
  • Who qualifies: Spouse and unmarried children of a veteran who is rated 100% Permanent and Total (P&T), OR of a veteran who died in the line of duty or from a service-connected disability
  • CHAMPVA vs. TRICARE: Dependents of 100% P&T veterans who are NOT eligible for TRICARE use CHAMPVA; if eligible for TRICARE (because sponsor has retired pay), TRICARE takes precedence
  • Medicare coordination: If a CHAMPVA beneficiary has Medicare Parts A and B, CHAMPVA acts as secondary — effectively covering most of what Medicare doesn't

How to Say It

  • "If you are rated 100% Permanent and Total, your spouse and any unmarried dependent children have health coverage through CHAMPVA. This is significant — private health insurance for a spouse can easily cost $700–$1,000 per month. CHAMPVA provides real coverage for very little cost."
  • "The numbers: after a $50 individual deductible, CHAMPVA pays 75 cents of every dollar of covered costs. Your family maximum out-of-pocket is capped at $3,000 per year per person. For many families, this is outstanding coverage."
  • "CHAMPVA is run by the VA Health Administration Center in Denver — it's separate from the regular VA healthcare system. Dependents don't go to VA facilities; they use civilian providers who accept CHAMPVA."
  • "One important coordination rule: if your dependent is eligible for TRICARE (which they are if you're a military retiree with TRICARE access), they use TRICARE, not CHAMPVA. CHAMPVA is specifically for veterans who can't get TRICARE — primarily those whose disability rating is 100% P&T but who did not retire from military service, or survived a service-connected veteran."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "My rating just hit 100% P&T. How do I enroll my spouse in CHAMPVA?"

A: Apply through the VA Health Administration Center using VA Form 10-10d. Include your VA rating decision letter, your marriage certificate, and the dependent's proof of identity. Processing takes several months, so apply as soon as eligibility is established. The number is 1-800-733-8387.

Q: "Does CHAMPVA cover prescriptions?"

A: Yes. CHAMPVA covers prescription medications. There is also a mail-order pharmacy option through the Meds by Mail program, which provides generic medications at no cost for many routine prescriptions. This is a significant cost advantage.

Q: "If my spouse turns 65, do they lose CHAMPVA?"

A: No — CHAMPVA doesn't end at 65 if the veteran's P&T status continues. However, at 65, if your spouse enrolls in Medicare Parts A and B, CHAMPVA becomes a secondary payer — it covers most of the costs Medicare doesn't. Enrolling in Medicare Part B is strongly encouraged at 65 because of this cost-saving coordination.

Medicare at Age 65

What's on Screen

Medicare overview: Parts A, B, C, and D — what each covers, enrollment windows, and the key enrollment deadlines veterans need to know.

Key Numbers

  • Part A (Hospital): Premium-free for most people with 40+ quarters of work history; covers inpatient hospital, skilled nursing, hospice
  • Part B (Medical): $202.90/month standard premium for 2026; covers outpatient care, doctor visits, preventive services
  • IRMAA threshold (2026): $109,000 single / $218,000 married filing jointly — income above these levels triggers surcharges on Part B and Part D premiums (based on income 2 years prior)
  • Part D (Prescription): Varies by plan; late enrollment penalty applies if you delay and don't have creditable coverage
  • Initial Enrollment Period: 7-month window: 3 months before, the month of, and 3 months after your 65th birthday
  • Late enrollment penalty (Part B): 10% per 12-month period of delay — permanent, added to premium for life

How to Say It

  • "Medicare is not automatic — you have to enroll. And the window matters. You have a 7-month window around your 65th birthday to sign up without penalty. If you miss it without having other qualifying coverage, you pay a permanent surcharge on your Part B premium for every year you delayed. That 10% per year adds up fast."
  • "Part A is free for almost everyone who's worked 40 or more quarters — that's 10 years. Part B costs $202.90 per month in 2026 for most people. This premium is deducted automatically from your Social Security check once you're enrolled in both."
  • "There's an income-based surcharge called IRMAA. If your income in 2024 was above $109,000 (single) or $218,000 (married filing jointly), your 2026 Part B premium is higher. IRMAA is calculated on income two years prior — important to know if you had a high-income year from a large TSP withdrawal or property sale."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "I have TRICARE for Life. Do I still need Medicare Part B?"

A: Yes — you must enroll in Medicare Parts A and B to keep TRICARE for Life. TRICARE for Life is designed as a Medicare wraparound plan. It pays what Medicare doesn't. But if you drop Part B, you lose TRICARE for Life as well. This is non-negotiable: enroll in both Parts A and B at 65 to maintain TFL.

Q: "I'm still working at 65. Do I need to enroll in Medicare?"

A: If you are covered by a group health plan from your (or your spouse's) active employment — not retirement — you generally can delay Medicare enrollment without penalty. But be careful about how VA healthcare interacts with Medicare's special enrollment period rules. This is worth a specific conversation with Medicare at 1-800-633-4227 before you turn 65.

Q: "What is IRMAA and can I appeal it?"

A: IRMAA (Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount) is a higher Medicare premium for higher-income beneficiaries, based on your income from 2 years ago. If your income has dropped significantly since that year — due to retirement, loss of a spouse, or other life event — you can request a reconsideration using SSA Form SSA-44. IRMAA is appealable and many people successfully get it adjusted.

TRICARE for Life

What's on Screen

TRICARE for Life (TFL) — how it works as a Medicare wraparound, what it covers, and what it costs compared to other TRICARE plans.

Key Numbers

  • TFL eligibility: Military retirees and their dependents who have Medicare Parts A and B
  • TFL premium: No additional TRICARE premium; your only cost is the Medicare Part B premium ($202.90/mo in 2026)
  • How it works: Medicare pays first; TFL pays most or all of what Medicare doesn't cover (deductibles, copays, coinsurance)
  • Out-of-pocket costs with TFL + Medicare: Generally very close to zero for most covered services
  • Pharmacy: TFL beneficiaries have access to TRICARE pharmacy network and mail-order (Express Scripts) for reduced-cost prescriptions
  • Requirement: Must maintain Medicare Part B enrollment to keep TFL — if you drop Part B, you lose TFL

How to Say It

  • "If you are a military retiree and you enroll in Medicare Parts A and B when you turn 65, you automatically get TRICARE for Life. You don't have to apply — it's automatic. And it's an exceptional benefit."
  • "Here's how it works: you see any provider who accepts Medicare. Medicare pays its share. TRICARE for Life then pays almost everything Medicare didn't cover. Your out-of-pocket cost on most services is close to zero. Your only ongoing cost is the Medicare Part B premium."
  • "This is one of the best healthcare arrangements available to any retiree in America. The $202.90 Medicare Part B premium buys you, effectively, near-zero-cost healthcare through the TFL coordination. Compare that to a Medigap policy that might cost $150–$300/month on top of Part B."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "If I have TRICARE for Life, do I still need VA healthcare?"

A: These are two separate benefits. TFL covers all covered services through Medicare-accepting civilian providers. VA healthcare covers VA-eligible conditions, often with additional benefits for service-connected conditions. Many veterans use both: VA for service-connected conditions (which may be free or low cost), and TFL for general healthcare. There's no requirement to choose one over the other.

Q: "Does TRICARE for Life have a different pharmacy than regular TRICARE?"

A: TFL beneficiaries use the TRICARE pharmacy benefit, which includes the TRICARE Pharmacy at military treatment facilities (usually free for generics), the Express Scripts mail-order service (very low cost), and retail network pharmacies. The mail-order option is particularly valuable for maintenance medications. This is often cheaper than running prescriptions through Medicare Part D.

Q: "I had a big year of income two years ago — can I do anything about this year's IRMAA?"

IRMAA can be adjusted prospectively based on current-year estimated income. Contact your Social Security office to start this process.

  • Dental and vision: None of the three programs provides routine dental/vision by default — TRICARE Dental, FEDVIP, or separate plans needed
  • Long-term care: VA Aid & Attendance (covered next); Medicare does NOT cover custodial long-term care

How to Say It

  • "This table is about helping you understand what you have — not choosing between them. Most of you in this room are eligible for all three. The goal is to use them strategically."
  • "Here's the rough strategy most veterans use: VA healthcare for service-connected conditions — because it's free or low-cost and the VA knows those conditions best. Medicare plus TRICARE for Life for everything else — because the combination essentially provides near-universal civilian coverage. You're not forced to choose one over another."
  • "The gap that surprises most retirees: dental and vision. None of these programs covers routine dental or vision automatically. You need TRICARE Dental, FEDVIP, or a private plan. Dental care in retirement is a significant and often underestimated expense."
  • "Long-term care — nursing home care, home health aides, assisted living — Medicare covers none of this beyond a very short skilled nursing stay after hospitalization. This is one of the most significant financial risks in retirement, and we'll talk about VA's Aid & Attendance benefit on the next few slides."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Can I use both VA healthcare and Medicare at the same time?"

A: Yes. They are completely separate programs and there is no rule preventing you from using both. A common approach: use VA for service-connected conditions and VA pharmacy for those prescriptions, and use Medicare + TFL for non-VA conditions. You just can't bill both for the same service — each claim goes to one payer.

Q: "Does VA cover dental care?"

A: VA dental care is available, but eligibility is more limited than general VA healthcare. Veterans with service-connected dental conditions, 100% disability rating, or who are enrolled in certain VA vocational programs are eligible for VA dental care. Most veterans without those qualifications are not eligible for VA dental — and should look at FEDVIP or a commercial dental plan.

VA Aid & Attendance Overview

What's on Screen

Introduction to VA Aid & Attendance (A&A) — a supplemental benefit for veterans or surviving spouses who need help with Activities of Daily Living (ADLs), paid on top of the VA Pension.

Key Numbers

  • What it is: A higher VA Pension rate paid to veterans (or surviving spouses) who require the regular assistance of another person, are housebound, or are in a nursing home
  • Not a separate benefit: A&A is the maximum tier of VA Pension — the veteran must qualify for VA Pension first
  • Net worth requirement: Same $163,699 limit as VA Pension (2026)
  • 3-year look-back: Same asset transfer penalty as VA Pension
  • Use of funds: Flexible — can be used for home care, assisted living, adult day programs, nursing home, or any other legitimate care expense
  • Application: Through the VA — recommend using a VSO, VA-accredited attorney, or VA-accredited claims agent (not a fee-charging unlicensed agent)

How to Say It

  • "Aid and Attendance is one of the most underutilized veterans benefits. Many families don't know it exists until they're already in a crisis — the veteran is in a nursing home, money is running out, and someone finally asks about veterans benefits."
  • "Here's the core concept: A&A is the maximum rate of the VA Pension benefit, paid to veterans who need help with basic daily activities. If you need help bathing, dressing, eating, getting to the toilet, or you're essentially bedridden — you likely qualify for the A&A level."
  • "The money is flexible. Unlike some government benefits, you're not told exactly how to spend it. Home care, assisted living, adult day programs — all valid. This is cash-value help."
  • "The key gatekeepers: you need to meet the income and net worth thresholds, have wartime service, and demonstrate the need for ADL assistance. We'll go through the specific rates on the next slide and the 5 ADLs on Slide 8."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Can a veteran living at home qualify, or does this only apply in a nursing home?"

A: Home-based veterans absolutely qualify — being in a nursing home is not required. The standard is whether you need assistance with ADLs, not where you live. Many A&A recipients live at home with a paid caregiver, family member, or home health aide. The benefit helps fund that care.

Q: "Does this affect Medicaid eligibility?"

A: VA A&A and Medicaid are separate programs with separate rules. The interaction can be complex — particularly around how A&A income is counted toward Medicaid eligibility. This is a detailed planning question that requires a VA-accredited attorney or elder law attorney who understands both benefit systems.

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "What counts as 'countable income' for the A&A calculation?"

A: Countable income for VA Pension purposes includes most income: military retirement pay, Social Security, interest, dividends, rental income. VA disability compensation is excluded from countable income. Also importantly, unreimbursed medical expenses — including the cost of in-home care — can be deducted from countable income, which can significantly increase the benefit. This is an area where a VSO can help you maximize your eligible benefit.

Q: "Is this $2,424 per month on top of VA disability compensation?"

A: Potentially, but the rules are complex. VA Pension (of which A&A is the maximum tier) and VA disability compensation are generally not paid simultaneously. A veteran who is eligible for both typically receives whichever is higher. This is an area where a VSO should run the numbers for your specific situation — it's not a simple yes/no answer.

Eligibility & the 5 ADLs

What's on Screen

Key Numbers

How to Say It

  • "The 5 ADLs are the functional measurement the VA uses to determine whether a veteran or surviving spouse actually needs the level of care that A&A covers. These are basic tasks: bathing yourself, getting dressed, eating, going to the bathroom, getting in and out of bed or a chair."
  • "If a veteran needs regular help with even one of these — or is essentially bedridden, in a nursing home, or has severe visual impairment — they meet the clinical standard. The threshold is lower than people think."
  • "The doctor's statement on VA Form 21-2680 is critical. It needs to specifically address the ADL limitations. A note that says 'patient has difficulty with daily activities' is not enough. The form needs to specify what the patient can and cannot do independently."

Facilitation tip: Many men in the room will be reluctant to admit they or someone they love needs ADL help. Frame this in practical terms: "This isn't charity. It's a benefit that was earned. The question is whether you're going to use what belongs to you."
Redirect: If the discussion goes to "how do I get someone rated to qualify?" that's the right question — redirect to: "Talk to a VSO. They'll review the documentation, help you complete Form 21-2680 with the right physician, and submit a clean application. It is always free."

Federal Taxation

What's on Screen

Federal tax treatment of each income source — pension, VA comp, SS, TSP, Roth — with the Social Security provisional income formula and current tax brackets for 2026.

Key Numbers

  • VA disability comp: Federal tax-free — always, regardless of amount
  • Military retirement pay: Ordinary income — same brackets as wages; taxed as if it were employment income
  • Social Security — "combined income" formula: Combined income = Adjusted Gross Income + Non-taxable interest + 50% of Social Security benefits. Above $34,000 single / $44,000 married, up to 85% of SS is taxable.

How to Say It

  • "Let's walk through what the IRS actually does with each of your income streams. VA disability comp: the IRS doesn't touch it. It's not on your tax return at all. Military pension: taxed just like a paycheck — it's in Box 2a of your DFAS 1099-R and goes straight onto your 1040."
  • "Social Security is where people get surprised. Most people think 'Social Security is tax-free.' It's not — at least not for most retirees with other income. Q: "What about state income taxes on my pension or VA comp?"

Q: "How do I know if I'm withholding enough from my pension and Social Security?"

Debt in Retirement: The Reality

What's on Screen

Data on debt among retirees: the prevalence of mortgage debt, credit card debt, and car loans among veterans in retirement — and the income math of carrying high-interest debt on a fixed income.

Key Numbers

  • Percentage of retirees carrying debt: Research consistently shows 50%+ of Americans enter retirement with some form of debt
  • Credit card interest: Average credit card APR currently exceeds 20% — carrying a balance at that rate is one of the worst financial positions in retirement
  • Mortgage in retirement: Common and not always problematic — but needs to be sized correctly relative to fixed income
  • Debt-to-income ratio in retirement: Ideally below 36% of gross income — above that, flexibility disappears fast

How to Say It

  • "Debt in retirement is a real issue — not a personal failure — but something to manage deliberately. On a fixed income, every dollar of interest you pay is a dollar that can't cover healthcare, food, or anything else."
  • "The most damaging debt in retirement is credit card debt at 20%+ interest. On a $5,000 balance at 20%, you're paying $1,000 per year just in interest. That $1,000 could be meaningful. Priority one: eliminate high-interest revolving debt before anything else."
  • "Mortgage debt is different — it's secured, often at a much lower rate, and the interest may be deductible. That test is worth applying."
  • "One thing I see that costs veterans real money: using TSP or IRA funds to pay off debt. On the surface it looks like a good idea — eliminate a 20% debt. But when you add the income tax on that withdrawal, plus potentially the 10% early penalty, you've effectively paid 30–40% of that balance to the IRS. In many cases, the debt payoff costs more than the interest would have."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Is it worth paying off my mortgage before I retire?"

A: It depends on your interest rate, your other liquid assets, and your tax situation. A mortgage at 3% may make less financial sense to pay off early than one at 7%. There's also a psychological benefit to having no mortgage in retirement that isn't captured in pure financial math. This is a personal calculation worth running with a financial advisor who knows your complete picture.

Q: "Should I take a lump sum from my TSP to pay off my house?"

A: This is a significant personal financial decision I can't make for you. Key factors: the tax hit on a large TSP withdrawal can be substantial, potentially pushing you into a much higher bracket for that year. The elimination of housing debt provides cash flow and security. There's no universally right answer. Run the numbers with a financial advisor.

Managing Required Minimum Distributions

What's on Screen

Deep dive on RMDs: calculation method, the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table, common pitfalls, and strategies for minimizing RMD impact on taxes (Roth conversions, QCDs).

Key Numbers

  • RMD start age — born 1951–1959: 73
  • RMD start age — born 1960 or later: 75
  • RMD penalty for failure to take: 25% of the amount that should have been withdrawn
  • RMD calculation: Prior year-end account balance ÷ IRS Uniform Lifetime Table factor for your age
  • Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD): Up to $105,000/year from an IRA directly to charity — counts as RMD but excluded from taxable income; available age 70½+
  • Roth IRA: No RMD requirement during owner's lifetime; Roth TSP has RMDs but can be rolled to Roth IRA to avoid them
  • Roth conversion: Pre-RMD years can be used to convert traditional IRA/TSP to Roth, reducing future RMD amounts (taxable in year of conversion)

How to Say It

  • "RMDs are the IRS saying: you've been deferring taxes for decades. Time to pay up. The government requires you to start withdrawing — and therefore paying taxes on — your traditional retirement accounts when you reach RMD age."
  • "The penalty for missing an RMD is 25% of the amount you should have taken. If you should have taken $15,000 and didn't, you owe $3,750 — plus the regular income tax on the distribution. The penalty drops to 10% if you correct the mistake within 2 years. Don't miss these."
  • "For those of you who don't need your RMD income, there are two excellent strategies. First, Qualified Charitable Distributions: if you're 70½ or older, you can send your RMD directly to charity from your IRA — it counts toward your RMD but is excluded from your income. Second, Roth conversions in lower-income years before RMDs start — pay the tax now at a lower rate, reduce future RMDs, and leave tax-free money to heirs."
  • North Star reminder: "Whether a Roth conversion makes sense for you specifically depends on your current bracket, expected future income, state taxes, and how long the money will stay invested. This is a financial planning conversation, not something I can advise on for any individual."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Do I have to take RMDs from my Roth TSP?"

A: Technically yes — Roth TSP is subject to RMDs under the current rules for employer-sponsored plans. However, you can roll your Roth TSP to a Roth IRA at any time after separation, and Roth IRAs have no RMDs during the owner's lifetime. This is one reason many people roll their Roth TSP to a Roth IRA after leaving federal service.

Q: "My TSP account is large. Are my RMDs going to be huge?"

A: RMDs increase as you age because the IRS life expectancy factor decreases. If you have a large TSP balance, your RMDs in your late 70s and 80s can be substantial — potentially pushing you into a higher bracket and triggering IRMAA. This is exactly the situation where pre-RMD Roth conversions can be valuable. Discuss this with a tax professional.

Q: "Is 3% the right inflation number to use for planning?"

Q: "My pension is adjusted every year, so why do I need to worry about inflation?"

The concern is any fixed costs or expenses that grow faster than COLA, and any non-COLA income sources like a fixed annuity or stable G Fund returns. The goal is to make sure your total income and assets grow fast enough to cover a retirement that could last 20–30 years.

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Can I change my SBP election now?"

A: Changes after retirement are generally limited. You can add a spouse after marriage with a one-year window. There are limited statutory open seasons. If you believe your election was made without proper information — particularly if you were under undue influence or weren't properly counseled — consult a military attorney. Changes outside a qualifying event are very difficult.

Q: "Is SBP the same as life insurance?"

A: It's similar in purpose but different in structure. SBP pays a monthly annuity, not a lump sum. It inflates with COLA. It cannot be cashed out. It covers your beneficiary for life. Traditional life insurance pays a lump sum that must be managed and invested. Some retirees use SBP; others use private life insurance; some use both. Which is better depends on individual circumstances, health, and financial situation.

Q: "What happens to SBP if I get divorced?"

A: A divorce generally terminates the former spouse's SBP coverage unless there's a court order requiring former spouse coverage, or you elect former spouse coverage within one year of the divorce. A subsequent spouse can be added within one year of remarriage. SBP and divorce is complex territory — consult a military law attorney for your specific situation.

Premiums & Paid-Up Status

What's on Screen

SBP premium structure, tax deductibility, the paid-up SBP provision, and the lifetime cost analysis for a retiree who enrolled at retirement vs. the benefit the survivor receives.

Key Numbers

  • SBP premium rate: 6.5% of selected base amount — deducted from gross retirement pay before taxes
  • Paid-up status requirements: 360 months (30 years) of premium payments AND age 70 — BOTH conditions must be met
  • After paid-up status: No further premiums deducted; SBP coverage continues at full benefit level for the remainder of the retiree's life
  • Tax treatment of premiums: Pre-tax deduction from retirement pay — effectively reduces taxable income dollar-for-dollar
  • Survivor benefit: 55% of selected base, monthly, for life, with COLA
  • Survivor benefit taxation: Annuity received by the survivor is taxable ordinary income — but only the annuity amount, not a lump sum

How to Say It

  • "Let's talk about what SBP actually costs and when it stops costing. At 6.5% of your base, if your retirement pay is $3,000 per month, you're paying about $195 per month for SBP. Over 20 years, that's roughly $46,800 in premiums. Your survivor, if they outlive you by 10 years, receives $1,650 per month — totaling nearly $200,000 in annuity payments."
  • "There's a paid-up provision: once you've made 360 premium payments — that's 30 years — AND you've reached age 70, you stop paying premiums. Your coverage continues in full at no cost for the rest of your life. If you enrolled at 45 and retire with 30 years of service, paid-up status could happen in your mid-70s."
  • "The premiums are pre-tax, which softens the blow. If you're in a 22% tax bracket, a $195/month premium costs you about $152 after the tax savings."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "What happens if my spouse dies before me? Do I get my premiums back?"

A: If your spouse (who was the SBP beneficiary) dies before you, your SBP coverage terminates and your retirement pay increases by the amount of the premium deduction — you effectively stop paying. However, there is no refund of previously paid premiums. If you later remarry, you can add your new spouse within one year of marriage.

Q: "What if I die before reaching paid-up status? Was all that premium wasted?"

A: No — the purpose of SBP is to protect your survivor, not to be a savings vehicle. If you pay premiums for 10 years and then die, your survivor receives the full annuity for the rest of their life. The premiums were the cost of that insurance coverage. Think of it the same way you'd think of auto insurance — you don't say your annual premiums were "wasted" if you never had an accident.

Slide 3

What If You Declined SBP?

What's on Screen

Situation overview for veterans who declined SBP at retirement: what options (if any) remain, and how private life insurance or other financial planning can substitute.

Key Numbers

  • SBP open seasons: Congress has occasionally authorized limited open seasons for SBP enrollment. The most recent was in 2023. Watch for future opportunities through DFAS and military retiree associations.
  • VGLI (Veterans Group Life Insurance): Available to veterans transitioning from SGLI; maximum coverage $500,000; premiums increase with age; available up to age 79
  • Private life insurance: Term or permanent; health underwriting required; may not be available at older ages or with significant health conditions
  • Spousal self-funding approach: If SBP was declined, the alternative is ensuring sufficient liquid assets, life insurance, and/or other income sources exist to support the surviving spouse

How to Say It

  • "If you declined SBP at retirement, the options now are limited. Outside of a statutory open season — which Congress periodically authorizes but doesn't always do — there's no way to enroll after retirement without a qualifying life event."
  • "What you can do: ensure your estate plan accounts for your spouse's needs. This means looking at VGLI if you still have it, private life insurance if you're healthy enough to qualify, and making sure your surviving spouse would have enough income and assets to live on."
  • "The honest conversation in this room is: some of you may have declined SBP at retirement on the advice of someone who said you could 'do better' with private insurance or investments. That advice may or may not have worked out depending on what was actually done. This slide is not about regret — it's about making sure you've made a plan."

Facilitation Tips

Opening prompt: "Let's be candid here. How many of you elected SBP when you retired? [hands] How many of you declined? [hands] And for those who declined — do you have a plan for what happens to your spouse if you go first?" This question, asked gently, creates real engagement without embarrassment.
What good discussion looks like: Honest reflection. Someone saying "I declined it and I've regretted it" is valuable for the room — validate it without judgment: "That's a common feeling, and the important thing now is whether there's a plan in place." Someone saying "I declined it and I've got $800K in life insurance and a paid-off house" is also valid.
Redirect: Avoid any discussion of whether SBP was "smart" or "dumb" to decline at retirement — that decision is made and rehashing it helps no one. Keep the conversation forward-focused: "What's the plan from here?"
Time: 7–10 minutes. This slide generates deeply personal discussion. Be sensitive and forward-focused.
⚠ Watch-Out — SBP Advice Do not tell any veteran whether they should have elected SBP, or whether declining it was a mistake. These are highly personal decisions that depend on health, finances, alternative insurance, and spousal income. Your role is to explain the current situation and what options exist — not to assess the wisdom of past decisions.
Slide 4

Dependency & Indemnity Compensation (DIC)

What's on Screen

DIC overview: who qualifies, the current rate, how it interacts with SBP (the offset was eliminated in 2023), and important interaction with Social Security survivor benefits.

Key Numbers

  • 2026 DIC rate: $1,699.36 per month (effective December 1, 2025 — adjusted annually with SS COLA)
  • DIC eligibility: Surviving spouse of a veteran who died in service, from a service-connected disability, or who was rated 100% P&T for 10 or more continuous years immediately prior to death (the "10-year rule")
  • 10-year alternative rule: If rated 100% P&T for at least 5 years immediately following active duty, surviving spouse also eligible
  • SBP-DIC offset — ELIMINATED: As of January 1, 2023, the SBP-DIC offset is fully eliminated. Surviving spouses who receive DIC no longer have their SBP annuity reduced. Both are paid in full. This is a major change from prior law.
  • DIC tax treatment: Tax-free — same as VA disability compensation
  • DIC + Social Security survivor benefit: Both are payable simultaneously — DIC does not reduce SS survivor benefits

How to Say It

  • "DIC is a tax-free monthly benefit paid to the surviving spouse of a veteran who died from a service-connected condition, or who was rated 100% P&T for at least 10 consecutive years before death. In 2026, that rate is $1,699.36 per month."
  • "Here's the big policy change that many veterans don't know about yet: the SBP-DIC offset was fully eliminated as of January 1, 2023. Before that change, if a surviving spouse received DIC, their SBP annuity was reduced dollar-for-dollar. That is no longer the case. A surviving spouse who qualifies for both DIC and SBP now receives the full amount of both. This is a major financial improvement for surviving spouses."
  • "The 10-year rule is important to mention. Even if a veteran dies of a non-service-connected cause, if they were rated 100% P&T for 10 consecutive years immediately before death, their surviving spouse still qualifies for DIC. This is something 100% P&T veterans should make sure their spouses are aware of."
  • "DIC plus SBP plus Social Security survivor benefits can together provide a very substantial monthly income for a surviving spouse. Make sure your spouse knows about all three programs and knows to apply for all three after your death."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "My wife thought she could only get DIC or SBP, not both. Is that old information?"

A: Yes — that was the old rule. The SBP-DIC offset was fully eliminated effective January 1, 2023. Your spouse can now receive both the full SBP annuity and full DIC simultaneously. If a surviving spouse is currently receiving a reduced SBP annuity because of DIC, they should contact DFAS — they may be owed retroactive payments if the change hasn't been processed correctly.

Q: "How does my spouse apply for DIC after I die?"

A: Surviving spouses apply using VA Form 21P-534EZ. They should apply as soon as possible after the veteran's death. A VSO can assist with the application and make sure all supporting documentation is in order. The effective date is generally the first day of the month following the veteran's death if filed within one year.

Q: "Does DIC affect Social Security survivor benefits?"

A: No — DIC and Social Security survivor benefits are completely independent programs. Receiving DIC does not reduce Social Security survivor benefits in any way. The surviving spouse should apply for both.

Slide 5

Estate Essentials & VA Burial

What's on Screen

Core estate planning documents (will, power of attorney, healthcare directive), VA burial benefits (interment, marker, burial flag), and an overview of the National Cemetery Administration.

Key Numbers

  • VA burial benefits (for eligible veterans): Free interment at a national cemetery; free headstone or marker; free burial flag; free Presidential Memorial Certificate
  • Burial allowance: VA may pay a burial allowance for service-connected death (currently up to $2,000) or for veterans receiving VA pension/compensation at time of death (up to $948)
  • National cemeteries in or near WV: Grafton National Cemetery (Grafton, WV) and Pvt. Felix Longoria National Cemetery (Three Rivers, TX) are examples — check the NCA directory at cem.va.gov for nearest facility
  • Core documents needed: Last will and testament; Durable Power of Attorney (financial); Healthcare Advance Directive (living will + healthcare proxy)
  • Costs without planning: Probate costs, estate administration delays, and potential family conflicts if documents are absent

How to Say It

  • "Let's talk about two practical things in this slide: what your estate plan needs to include, and what the VA provides at end of life."
  • "On VA burial: if you served on active duty and received anything other than a dishonorable discharge, you are eligible for burial in a national cemetery at no cost. This includes the gravesite, opening and closing of the grave, a headstone or marker, and a burial flag. These are meaningful honors and meaningful cost savings. Tell your family now — so they know to call the National Cemetery Scheduling Office when the time comes."
  • "On estate documents: three documents are the minimum. A will tells the court who gets what. A Durable Power of Attorney lets a trusted person manage your finances if you can't — without going to court. A Healthcare Advance Directive tells doctors what you want if you can't speak for yourself, and names someone to make medical decisions for you."
  • "Without these documents, your state decides who manages your affairs, and it happens slowly and expensively through probate. A basic will, POA, and healthcare directive can be prepared by an attorney for a few hundred dollars. Legal aid services and military legal assistance offices can sometimes help at reduced or no cost."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Can my spouse be buried with me in a national cemetery?"

A: Yes. Spouses and dependent children of eligible veterans may be buried in national cemeteries, often in the same gravesite. There is no charge for the spouse's burial or marker in a national cemetery. This is a significant benefit that many families don't know about.

Q: "What is the difference between a will and a trust?"

A: A will goes through probate — it's a court-supervised process that can take months to years and can be contested. A trust transfers assets outside of probate — directly to beneficiaries, faster, and privately. Trusts are generally more expensive to set up but can save time and cost later. For simple estates, a will may be sufficient; for larger or more complex estates, a trust often makes sense. This is a decision for an estate planning attorney.

Slide 6

Beneficiary Designation Checklist

What's on Screen

A checklist of accounts and policies that require active beneficiary designations: TSP (Form TSP-3), life insurance (SGLI/VGLI), IRAs, bank accounts (POD designations), and brokerage accounts — with notes on why these override a will.

Key Numbers

  • TSP beneficiary form: TSP-3 — if none on file, TSP uses its own default order (spouse first, then children, then parents, then estate)
  • VGLI beneficiary: Managed by OSGLI (Office of Servicemembers' Group Life Insurance) — must be updated directly
  • IRA beneficiaries: Filed with the custodian (TSP, bank, brokerage) — not the will — these override a will completely
  • POD / TOD designations: "Payable on Death" (bank accounts) and "Transfer on Death" (brokerage) allow direct transfer without probate — free to set up at your bank or broker
  • Life insurance policies: Must be updated directly with the insurer — a will naming a different person does NOT override the policy designation

How to Say It

  • "This is the slide that causes the most preventable family crises. Beneficiary designations override your will. Period. If your TSP says your money goes to your first wife and you've been remarried for 20 years, that's where it goes — regardless of what your will says."
  • "Let me say that again: your will does NOT control your TSP, your IRA, your life insurance, or any account with a beneficiary designation. Those go directly to whoever is named on the form, bypassing the will entirely."
  • "The checklist on screen is what you need to audit. Every one of these needs a current, intentional beneficiary designation. 'Current' means reviewed after every major life event: marriage, divorce, birth of a child, death of a named beneficiary."

Facilitation Tips

Opening prompt: "Quick show of hands — how many of you know, off the top of your head, who is named as beneficiary on your TSP right now? Not a guess — you've actually checked recently." Very few hands go up. "That's exactly why we're talking about this."
What good discussion looks like: Specific stories of beneficiary designation mistakes — the veteran who never updated after divorce, the account that went to a deceased parent because the form was 30 years old, the children who discovered an ex-spouse on the life insurance. These stories are powerful and real. Share them factually, not as horror stories.
Action step to give: "Before this course ends today, I want you to write down two things you're going to do this week: check your TSP beneficiary form at TSP.gov, and pull out your most recent life insurance policy and check who's named. That's your homework."
Redirect: If someone asks about estate tax: "Federal estate tax currently only applies to estates over $13 million, so for most of us in this room, it's not a concern. State estate taxes vary — West Virginia does not have one. Focus on the beneficiary designations — that's where the real everyday planning lives."
Time: 7–10 minutes. This discussion is practical and action-oriented — keep the energy focused on the "what to do" side.
Slide 7

Protecting Your Estate

What's on Screen

Estate protection strategies: avoiding probate, titling assets correctly, the role of trusts, and special considerations for veterans with blended families or complex asset situations.

Key Numbers

  • Federal estate tax exemption (2026): Approximately $13.6 million per individual — federal estate tax doesn't affect most veterans
  • West Virginia estate/inheritance tax: None — WV does not have a state estate or inheritance tax
  • Probate avoidance methods: Beneficiary designations, joint tenancy with right of survivorship (JTWROS), revocable living trusts, POD/TOD designations — all transfer assets outside probate
  • Special needs considerations: If a beneficiary has a disability and receives government means-tested benefits (Medicaid, SSI), inheriting directly can disqualify them — a Special Needs Trust protects them

How to Say It

  • "Protecting your estate is really about two things: making sure your assets go where you intend them to, and getting them there as efficiently as possible — without unnecessary taxes, delays, or family disputes."
  • "For most veterans in this room, federal estate tax is not a concern — the exemption is over $13 million. West Virginia has no estate or inheritance tax. Your primary estate planning concern is not tax minimization; it's making sure your assets transfer cleanly to the right people."
  • "Joint tenancy, POD designations, and beneficiary forms are the easiest ways to move assets outside probate. A house titled jointly with a right of survivorship transfers automatically to the surviving owner when one dies — no probate. Bank accounts with POD designations work the same way."
  • "Blended families and second marriages add complexity. If you have children from a previous marriage and a current spouse, think carefully about how you want your assets divided — because joint tenancy with a current spouse may effectively cut out your children from a prior marriage. An estate planning attorney can help you structure this intentionally."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "Do I need a trust, or is a will enough?"

A: It depends on the size and complexity of your estate, whether you have minor children, and whether avoiding probate is important to you. For a simple estate where most assets have beneficiary designations and there are no complicated family situations, a will plus POD/TOD designations may be sufficient. For larger or more complex situations, a revocable living trust often makes sense. An estate planning attorney can advise you on what's appropriate.

Q: "What happens if I die without a will in West Virginia?"

A: West Virginia intestate succession law determines who inherits — generally your spouse and then children in specified proportions, and further to other relatives if you have no spouse or children. The distribution may or may not match what you would have wanted. It also takes longer and costs more through the probate court process. Having a will is always better.

Slide 8

Planning for Cognitive Decline

What's on Screen

Practical financial planning for cognitive decline: the importance of a Durable Power of Attorney, VA fiduciary program, financial red flags, and how to set up protections before they're needed.

Key Numbers

  • Dementia prevalence: Approximately 1 in 9 Americans over 65; higher risk with TBI history, which is significant in a veteran population
  • VA fiduciary program: If VA determines a beneficiary cannot manage their own VA benefits, they appoint a fiduciary to manage those funds — the beneficiary still retains rights and can challenge the appointment
  • Durable POA: "Durable" means it remains in effect if you become incapacitated — standard POA terminates at incapacity, which is when you need it most
  • Guardianship / conservatorship: Court-supervised alternative to POA when someone is already incapacitated and has no POA in place — expensive, slow, and removes autonomy
  • Financial exploitation: Elders with cognitive impairment are significantly more vulnerable to financial scams and exploitation by family members and strangers alike

How to Say It

  • "This is a topic nobody wants to talk about, but it's one of the most important slides in this course. Cognitive decline — whether from dementia, TBI, or other causes — is a real risk in our demographic. Veterans with TBI history are at elevated risk. This is not hypothetical."
  • "The key protective tool is a Durable Power of Attorney — and I want to emphasize the word 'durable.' A standard POA terminates the moment you become incapacitated. A durable POA remains effective precisely when you need it most. Without one, your family would have to go to court to get a guardianship to manage your affairs — an expensive, slow, court-supervised process."
  • "The VA has a fiduciary program. If the VA determines you can no longer manage your own VA benefits — and they make this determination through their own process — they will appoint a fiduciary. Having your own trusted person already named in a Durable POA is preferable to having the VA appoint someone you didn't choose."
  • "Practical steps to take now while you're fully capable: set up a Durable POA with someone you trust, set up your banking apps and accounts to allow a trusted family member as a secondary authorized user for monitoring, consider direct deposit and automated bill pay to reduce manual financial tasks as you age."

Anticipated Q&A

Q: "If I have a Durable POA, can that person take all my money?"

A: A POA agent is legally a fiduciary — they must act in your interest, not their own. Misuse of POA authority is elder financial abuse and is a crime. That said, it's critical to name someone you absolutely trust, and many attorneys recommend naming co-agents who must act together, or requiring periodic accounting. The level of protection depends on how the document is written and who you choose.

Q: "How does the VA fiduciary program work?"

A: If VA receives a report or evidence that a beneficiary cannot manage their own VA funds, they conduct a field examination. If they determine management is needed, they appoint a fiduciary — often a family member, but it can be a professional or VA employee. The fiduciary must file annual accountings. The beneficiary can request a change of fiduciary or appeal the determination. Having a trusted named Durable POA in place can influence this process in your favor.
