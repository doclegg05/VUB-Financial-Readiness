Module 1

Retirement Pay & TSP

8 Slides

Slide 1

Title & Objectives — Understanding Your Retirement Pay & TSP

Key Facts & Numbers

  • This module covers two things: how military retirement pay is calculated (High-3 vs. BRS) and how the Thrift Savings Plan works.
  • Most students in the room will be under the High-3 system. BRS applies to service members who entered after January 1, 2018, or who opted in during the 2018 enrollment window.
  • The four learning objectives are listed on screen. Use them to set expectations for the module.

How to Explain It

  • Open with: "This module is about the two main financial pillars of military retirement — your pension and your TSP. Most of you are already receiving these, so think of this as a check-up to make sure you're getting the most out of them."
  • Acknowledge that some students may have already separated years ago. The content is still relevant because TSP decisions and tax implications are ongoing.

Anticipated Questions

Q: "I retired 15 years ago. Does any of this apply to me?"

A: Absolutely. Your TSP is still there unless you withdrew it entirely. And understanding how your retirement pay interacts with taxes and other benefits is always relevant.

Q: "What if I was under the old Final Pay system?"

A: Final Pay applied to those who entered before September 8, 1980. It uses your final basic pay rather than the highest 36-month average. The formula is otherwise similar: final pay x 2.5% x years of service.

Slide 2

Military Retirement Pay

Key Facts & Numbers

  • High-3 formula: Average of highest 36 months of basic pay × 2.5% × years of service.
  • BRS formula: High-3 average × 2.0% × years of service, plus government TSP match (up to 5% of basic pay).
  • Example (High-3): 20 years at $7,000/month High-3 average = $3,500/month.
  • Example (BRS): Same numbers = $2,800/month pension + accumulated TSP match.
  • COLA: Military retirement pay is adjusted annually using the Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners (CPI-W). This protects purchasing power over time.

How to Explain It

  • Start simple: "Your retirement pay is basically a percentage of your pay for every year you served. Under High-3, that percentage is 2.5% per year. So 20 years gets you 50% of your average highest pay."
  • For BRS, explain: "BRS lowers the multiplier to 2%, but the government contributes to your TSP on top of that. If you opted into BRS, your pension check is smaller but your TSP should be larger."
  • Emphasize COLA: "One of the best features of military retirement is that it keeps up with inflation. Social Security does this too, but many private pensions do not."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "How do I find out what my High-3 average actually is?"

A: Your Retiree Account Statement on myPay shows your pay base. DFAS calculated it at retirement from your last 36 months of basic pay. If you need the exact calculation, contact DFAS directly.

Q: "Does the COLA increase every year?"

A: It is calculated every year but the amount varies. In some years (like 2015) it was 0%. In 2022 it was 8.7%. It tracks the same index as Social Security COLA.

Q: "Can I get more than 50% if I served more than 20 years?"

A: Yes, absolutely. Each additional year adds 2.5% (High-3). So 24 years = 60%, 30 years = 75%. The maximum is 75% of your pay base.

Slide 3

Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) Overview

Key Facts & Numbers

  • G Fund: Government Securities — lowest risk, guaranteed not to lose value. Returns are modest (around 4-5% historically).
  • F Fund: Fixed Income / Bonds — low-moderate risk. Tracks the Bloomberg Barclays U.S. Aggregate Bond Index.
  • C Fund: Common Stock — tracks the S&P 500. Moderate risk, historically ~10% average annual return over long periods.
  • S Fund: Small Cap Stock — higher risk, higher potential return. Tracks smaller U.S. companies not in the S&P 500.
  • I Fund: International Stock — higher risk. Tracks developed international markets.
  • L Funds (Lifecycle): Target-date funds that automatically shift from aggressive (stocks) to conservative (bonds/G fund) as you approach your target retirement year.

How to Explain It

  • Use an analogy: "Think of the TSP like a cafeteria with five main dishes. G is the safest — like putting money under your mattress, but it still earns a little. C is the most popular — it follows the 500 biggest companies in America. S and I add more variety but more ups and downs."
  • Emphasize L Funds for those who are hands-off: "If you don't want to think about which funds to pick, the L Funds do it for you. You just pick the one closest to your age or retirement year."
  • Many students may have their entire TSP in the G Fund. Gently note that while safe, this may not keep up with inflation over a long retirement.

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Is the G Fund safe? Can I lose money in it?"

A: The G Fund has never had a negative return. However, if its return is lower than inflation, you lose purchasing power over time. It is safe in terms of principal but may not grow fast enough for a 20-30 year retirement.

Q: "Can I change my fund allocation?"

A: Yes, you can change your allocation at any time through TSP.gov. There is no fee for interfund transfers. You get two free transfers per month, and unlimited transfers into the G Fund.

Q: "I separated years ago and forgot about my TSP. Is it still there?"

A: If you had a balance over $200, your account stayed open. Log in at TSP.gov or call the ThriftLine at 1-877-968-3778. If you cannot access it, DFAS or your service branch can help locate it.

Slide 4

TSP Withdrawal Options

Key Facts & Numbers

  • TSP Monthly Payments: Set a fixed dollar amount or choose life-expectancy-based payments calculated by the TSP.
  • Single Withdrawal: One-time partial withdrawal, minimum $1,000.
  • Full Withdrawal: Lump sum, annuity purchase through MetLife, or rollover/transfer to an IRA.
  • Leave It: TSP grows tax-deferred until Required Minimum Distribution age.
  • RMD age: 73 for most current retirees. Rises to 75 for those born after 1959 under the SECURE 2.0 Act.
  • Early withdrawal penalty: 10% penalty on withdrawals before age 59 1/2, UNLESS you separated from service in the calendar year you turned 55 or later (the "Rule of 55").
  • Tax treatment: Traditional TSP withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income. Roth TSP withdrawals are tax-free if the account has been open 5+ years and you are over 59 1/2.

How to Explain It

  • "You have four basic choices with your TSP: take monthly payments like a paycheck, take a one-time chunk, take everything out, or leave it alone and let it grow."
  • "The smartest move for many retirees is to leave it growing as long as you can, especially if you have other income covering your expenses. The money compounds tax-deferred."
  • On the penalty: "If you separated at 55 or older, you can withdraw from TSP without the 10% early penalty. This is a special rule that doesn't apply to IRAs, so think carefully before rolling your TSP into an IRA if you're between 55 and 59 1/2."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Should I roll my TSP into an IRA?"

A: It depends. IRAs offer more investment choices, but TSP has extremely low fees (around 0.04% expense ratio). If you are between 55 and 59 1/2, rolling to an IRA could cost you the penalty-free withdrawal benefit. Recommend consulting a financial advisor before making this move.

Q: "What happens to my TSP if I die?"

A: It goes to whoever is listed as your beneficiary on TSP Form TSP-3. If no form is on file, it follows the TSP default order: spouse, then children, then parents, then estate. Make sure your form is current.

Q: "What is an RMD exactly?"

A: A Required Minimum Distribution is the amount the IRS requires you to withdraw from tax-deferred accounts each year starting at age 73 (or 75 if born after 1959). The amount is based on your account balance and life expectancy. If you don't take it, there is a 25% penalty on the amount you should have withdrawn.

Discussion — The TSP Decision

Facilitation Tips

Opening the conversation: Read the prompt on screen, then pause. Let silence work for you — someone will speak up. If not, start with: "Raise your hand if you still have a TSP account. Now keep your hand up if you know which funds it's invested in." This usually gets a laugh and opens the floor.
What good answers look like: You want students sharing real experiences — "I left mine in G Fund for 10 years and wish I hadn't" or "I rolled mine into an IRA because I wanted more options." These personal stories are more valuable than textbook answers.
Follow-up prompts on screen:
  • "Do you know which funds your TSP is currently invested in?"
  • "Have you considered how your TSP withdrawal strategy affects your taxes?"
  • "If you could advise a younger veteran about their TSP, what would you say?"
Redirecting: If someone starts giving specific investment advice ("You should put everything in the C Fund"), gently redirect: "That's one perspective, and it shows you're thinking about growth. Everyone's situation is different though — risk tolerance, timeline, other income — so it's worth talking to a financial advisor before making big changes."
Time management: Aim for 5-7 minutes. Wrap up with: "The takeaway here is that your TSP is not a set-it-and-forget-it decision. Whether you leave it, draw from it, or move it, make that an active choice based on your situation."
Slide 6

Key Contacts & Resources

Key Facts & Numbers

  • VA.gov — Central hub for claims, healthcare enrollment, education benefits.
  • DFAS.mil — Defense Finance and Accounting Service. Manages retired pay, tax statements (1099-R), SBP, allotments.
  • TSP.gov — Account management, fund changes, withdrawal requests. ThriftLine: 1-877-968-3778.
  • myPay — DFAS self-service portal for pay stubs, W-2s, tax withholding changes, and direct deposit.
  • Local VSO — Veterans Service Organizations (DAV, VFW, American Legion) provide free claims assistance. They are accredited by the VA.
  • Military OneSource — Free financial counseling for eligible service members and retirees. 1-800-342-9647.
  • Veterans Crisis Line — 988, then press 1. Always worth mentioning in any veterans class.

How to Explain It

  • "I want you to walk away with these bookmarked or written down. These are the places that can actually help you with the things we're discussing today."
  • Emphasize VSOs: "A VSO is like having a free advocate who speaks the VA's language. If you have a claim or appeal pending, they can help at no cost."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Which VSO is best?"

A: They all provide the same core service — accredited claims assistance. It comes down to personal preference and local availability. DAV, VFW, and American Legion are the largest. Many VA medical centers also have VSO representatives on-site.

Q: "Is Military OneSource free for retirees?"

A: Military OneSource is available to active duty, Guard/Reserve, and their families. Retirees have more limited access. However, many of the same services are available through VA or community-based organizations. Check MilitaryOneSource.mil for current eligibility.

Module 2

Disability Pay & Compensation

12 Slides

Slide 1

Title & Objectives — Navigating Disability Pay & Compensation

Key Facts & Numbers

  • This module covers the VA Waiver (dollar-for-dollar offset), CRDP, CRSC, and how disability pay interacts with Social Security.
  • This is one of the most confusing areas in military benefits. Many veterans receive less than they are entitled to because they do not understand these programs.

How to Explain It

  • Open with: "This is the module where we untangle something that has confused veterans for decades. If you have a VA disability rating AND retired pay, you are affected by this. Let's make sure you understand how."
  • Set the tone that this is complex but important: "I'm going to walk through this step by step. If at any point something doesn't make sense, stop me."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "I have a VA disability rating but I'm not a military retiree. Does this apply to me?"

A: The VA Waiver, CRDP, and CRSC specifically apply to military retirees who also have a VA disability rating. If you're not receiving military retired pay, the offset doesn't affect you — you simply receive your VA disability compensation in full.

Slide 2

The VA Waiver (Offset)

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Federal law prohibits receiving full military retired pay and full VA disability compensation simultaneously (this goes back to the 19th century).
  • The "waiver" means retirees must give up (waive) a dollar of retired pay for every dollar of VA disability they receive.
  • Two programs exist to restore some or all of the waived amount: CRDP and CRSC.
  • You cannot receive both CRDP and CRSC. You must choose one annually.

How to Explain It

  • Use a concrete example: "Say your retired pay is $2,500/month and your VA disability compensation is $1,500/month. Without CRDP or CRSC, you would get $1,000 in retired pay and $1,500 in VA disability — the same $2,500 total. The VA money replaces your retired pay dollar for dollar."
  • "CRDP and CRSC exist to fix this. They give back some or all of that waived retired pay so you actually receive more total money."
  • Why it matters: "VA disability compensation is tax-free. Retired pay is taxable. The offset changes not just how much you get but what you owe in taxes."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Why can't I just get both?"

A: It is a long-standing federal law. Congress has partially fixed it through CRDP and CRSC, but the underlying rule remains. Many veteran advocacy groups continue to push for full concurrent receipt for all disabled retirees.

Q: "Does this affect my net pay?"

A: Your total gross amount stays roughly the same without CRDP/CRSC, but the tax picture changes because VA disability is tax-free and retired pay is taxable. With CRDP or CRSC, you actually receive more total money.

Slide 3

CRDP: Concurrent Retirement & Disability Pay

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Minimum rating: 50% VA disability.
  • Application: Automatic. No form required — DFAS enrolls eligible retirees.
  • Tax status: Taxable (treated as retired pay).
  • Effect: Restores retired pay that was waived due to the VA offset, so you effectively receive both your full retired pay and your full VA disability.

How to Explain It

  • "CRDP is the simpler of the two programs. If you have 50% or higher VA disability, DFAS automatically restores your waived retired pay. You don't need to fill out any forms."
  • "The catch is that the restored amount is taxable, just like your regular retired pay. So you get more money, but Uncle Sam takes a cut."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "I'm at 40% disability. Am I out of luck?"

A: For CRDP, yes — you need at least 50%. However, if any of your conditions are combat-related, you may qualify for CRSC at just 10%. Also, consider whether you have conditions that haven't been rated yet — a VSO can help with new claims or increases.

Q: "How do I know if I'm receiving CRDP?"

A: Check your Retiree Account Statement on myPay. CRDP will show as a separate line item. If you believe you are eligible but don't see it, contact DFAS.

Slide 4

CRSC: Combat-Related Special Compensation

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Minimum rating: 10% VA disability (but must be combat-related).
  • Application: Requires DD Form 2860, submitted to your service branch (not the VA, not DFAS).
  • Tax status: Non-taxable. This is a major advantage over CRDP.
  • Combat-related means: Injury/disability caused by direct combat, hazardous duty, an instrumentality of war, or conditions simulating war. Each condition must be individually justified.

How to Explain It

  • "CRSC is the tax-free option, but it requires more work on your part. You have to prove that each disability is combat-related, and you submit the form to your branch of service, not the VA."
  • "The key advantage: CRSC payments are not taxed. For someone in a 22% tax bracket, that can mean hundreds of dollars more per year in take-home pay compared to CRDP."
  • Emphasize: "Not every service-connected disability qualifies. You need a combat nexus — a direct connection between the condition and combat or hazardous military duty."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "I have hearing loss from firing ranges. Is that combat-related?"

A: Possibly. Hearing loss from weapons training can qualify as an "instrumentality of war" if it occurred during military training with weapons or combat-related equipment. Your service branch makes that determination based on your DD 2860 application.

Q: "Where do I get DD Form 2860?"

A: It is available online from your branch of service. You can also find it through a web search for "DD Form 2860." A VSO can help you complete it.

Slide 5

CRDP vs. CRSC: Side by Side

Key Facts & Numbers

  • This slide shows the two programs in parallel columns for easy comparison.
  • CRDP: 50% minimum, automatic, taxable, any service-connected disability.
  • CRSC: 10% minimum, requires DD 2860, non-taxable, combat-related only.

How to Explain It

  • "Here's the cheat sheet. The left column is CRDP — the easy, automatic one. The right column is CRSC — the one that takes more effort but gives you tax-free money."
  • "The question you need to answer for yourself is: do I have combat-related disabilities, and is the tax savings worth the paperwork?"
  • Practical advice: "If you qualify for both, DFAS will automatically pay you whichever is higher. But you should still apply for CRSC to give yourself the option, because your situation can change year to year."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Can I get CRDP for some conditions and CRSC for others?"

A: No. You elect one program or the other — it covers your entire disability offset. You cannot split conditions between the two programs.

Slide 7

Annual Election & DFAS Lags

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Annual election: Retirees who qualify for both CRDP and CRSC can switch between them once per year.
  • DFAS reporting lags: When the VA changes your disability rating retroactively, there can be a significant delay before DFAS adjusts your retired pay. This can result in unexpected debts (if you were overpaid) or back payments (if you were underpaid).
  • Practical advice: Monitor myPay closely after any rating change. Contact DFAS promptly if you see discrepancies.

How to Explain It

  • "Every year, DFAS gives you whichever program pays more. But if your situation changed — new rating, new combat-related determination — run the numbers both ways."
  • "The lag is real and frustrating. When the VA changes your rating, it can take weeks or months for DFAS to catch up. In the meantime, you might get overpaid or underpaid. Keep records of everything."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "What if DFAS overpaid me? Do I have to pay it back?"

A: Yes, generally DFAS will recoup overpayments by reducing future checks. However, you can request a waiver if repayment would cause financial hardship. Contact DFAS as soon as you notice the issue.

Q: "How do I switch between CRDP and CRSC?"

A: DFAS typically sends a letter or notification during the annual open season. You can also contact DFAS directly to request a change. A VSO can help with this process.

Slide 8

Social Security Interaction

Key Facts & Numbers

  • VA disability compensation is tax-free and does NOT count as earned income for Social Security purposes.
  • You CAN receive both VA disability and Social Security simultaneously — neither reduces the other.
  • Veterans who served 1957-2001 may receive up to $1,200/year in additional Social Security earnings credits (Special Extra Earnings credits).

How to Explain It

  • "Here's the good news: your VA disability check and your Social Security check are completely independent. Getting one does not reduce the other."
  • "And for those of you who served between 1957 and 2001, you may have extra Social Security credits you didn't know about. These were added automatically, but it's worth verifying with SSA."
  • Why it matters: "Some veterans avoid filing for VA disability because they think it will reduce their Social Security. It won't. This is one of those myths that costs people real money."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Does my military pension affect my Social Security?"

A: Military retirement pay does not reduce Social Security benefits. The Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) and Government Pension Offset (GPO) affect CIVIL SERVICE pensions (CSRS), not military pensions. If you also have a civil service pension under CSRS, that's a different conversation.

Q: "How do I check if I got those extra credits?"

A: Create an account at SSA.gov and review your earnings record. The special credits should already be applied. If your military service earnings look low, contact SSA with your DD-214.

Slide 9

The VA Pension Exception

Key Facts & Numbers

  • VA Pension (needs-based, for wartime veterans with limited income) IS affected by other income, including Social Security.
  • VA Disability Compensation (for service-connected conditions) is NOT affected by other income.
  • These are two completely different programs. Confusing them is a common and costly mistake.

How to Explain It

  • "This is a critical distinction. VA disability compensation and VA pension sound similar but work very differently."
  • "Disability compensation is based on your service-connected conditions. It doesn't matter how much other money you have. Pension is needs-based — it's for wartime veterans with low income who may or may not have service-connected conditions."
  • "If someone tells you that your Social Security will reduce your VA payment, ask which VA payment they mean. If it's disability compensation, they're wrong. If it's pension, they may be right."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Can I receive both VA pension and VA disability compensation?"

A: You cannot receive both simultaneously. The VA will pay whichever benefit is higher. In most cases, if you have a service-connected disability rating, compensation pays more than pension.

Slide 10

Discussion — CPL Johnson's Retirement Puzzle

Facilitation Tips

The scenario: CPL Johnson retired at E-7 with 22 years, has a 70% VA disability rating, and is about to turn 62. He is deciding whether to start Social Security now or wait until 67 (full retirement age).
Key factors to draw out:
  • His VA disability does NOT interact with Social Security — reinforce this.
  • Starting Social Security at 62 means a permanent ~30% reduction from his full retirement age benefit.
  • Waiting until 67 gives him the full benefit. Waiting until 70 adds delayed retirement credits (~8% per year).
  • His other income (retired pay + VA disability + possible CRDP) may be enough to cover expenses while he waits.
  • Spousal benefits: if married, his wife may be entitled to 50% of his full benefit at her full retirement age.
What good answers look like: Students weighing tradeoffs — "He should look at his total monthly income without SS and see if he can get by" or "His health matters — if he's not in great shape, maybe take it now."
Redirecting: If someone gives overly specific advice, remind the group: "This is a personal decision that depends on health, other income, marital status, and life expectancy. There's no one right answer."
Time: 7-10 minutes. This is a rich scenario. Close with: "The big point is that VA disability gives you breathing room to make the Social Security decision on your own terms."

How to Explain It

    3. CRSC: 10% minimum, DD 2860 required, non-taxable.
    4. You must elect one annually — they cannot be combined.
    5. VA disability does NOT reduce Social Security benefits.
  • Closing line: "Check your myPay statement. Know which program you're in. If you think you might qualify for CRSC, talk to a VSO."

Module 3

Healthcare & Long-Term Care

24 Slides | Suggested teaching time: 95-115 minutes

Expanded Plan

Week 3 Pacing Plan for a 1.5-2 Hour Class

Recommended Flow

  • 0-10 min: Reconnect from Week 2. Ask what changed in their understanding of VA disability, CRDP, CRSC, or Social Security after last class.
  • 10-30 min: VA healthcare foundation. Cover priority groups, access outside VA, CHAMPVA, and VA home/community supports.
  • 30-65 min: Medicare and TRICARE at 65. Spend the most time on the Initial Enrollment Period, Part B penalty, TRICARE for Life requirement, drug coverage, and Medicare Advantage caution.
  • 65-78 min: Healthcare scenario activity. Groups decide what three different people should do before age 65: a VA-only veteran, a military retiree, and a CHAMPVA spouse.
  • 78-105 min: Long-term care. Build from home supports to assisted living, skilled nursing, Aid & Attendance, documentation, and Medicaid interaction.

Anticipated Questions

Q: "I already have great VA coverage. Why do I need Medicare?"

Slide 2

VA Healthcare Priority Groups

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Group 1: 50%+ service-connected disability — no copays for any VA care.
  • Groups 2-3: 10-40% disability, former POWs, Purple Heart recipients — reduced copays.
  • Group 4: Catastrophically disabled veterans.
  • Group 5: Low-income veterans with no service-connected disability.
  • Groups 6-8: Various eligibility categories with increasing copays.
  • Priority group determines copay amounts and which services are available. Enrollment locks in your group.

How to Explain It

  • "The VA doesn't treat everyone the same. Your priority group determines what you pay and what services you can access. Group 1 is the best — no copays at all."
  • Key advice: "Enroll even if you're healthy. Your priority group is set when you enroll. If your health or finances change later, you're already in the system."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "How do I find out my priority group?"

A: Contact your local VA medical center enrollment coordinator, or check at VA.gov/health-care. Your enrollment letter should state your group.

Q: "Can my priority group change?"

A: Yes, if your disability rating changes or your income changes. A rating increase could move you to a higher priority group with better benefits.

Slide 3

CHAMPVA: Coverage for Dependents

Key Facts & Numbers

  • CHAMPVA covers dependents (spouse, children) of veterans who are permanently and totally disabled due to service, or who died from a service-connected disability.
  • Coverage: 75% of allowable charges.
  • Annual deductible: $50 per person, $100 per family.
  • Annual out-of-pocket cap: $3,000.
  • CHAMPVA is NOT TRICARE. It is a separate program administered by the VA, not the DoD.

How to Explain It

  • "CHAMPVA is for your family members, not for you. If you are permanently and totally disabled from your service, your spouse and kids can get healthcare coverage through CHAMPVA."
  • "It covers 75% of charges with a low deductible and a $3,000 annual cap. It's not as comprehensive as TRICARE, but it's a solid safety net."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "My spouse has her own insurance through work. Can she still use CHAMPVA?"

A: Yes. CHAMPVA can act as secondary insurance, covering costs that her primary insurance does not. She would need to be enrolled in both.

Q: "Does CHAMPVA cover prescriptions?"

A: Yes. CHAMPVA covers prescription drugs through the Meds by Mail program or at participating pharmacies with a copay.

Slide 4

Medicare at Age 65

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Veterans are strongly encouraged to enroll in Medicare Parts A and B at age 65.
  • VA care is strongest inside VA, with some authorized community, urgent, and emergency care. Medicare covers Medicare-accepting providers nationwide.
  • For military retirees with TRICARE: enrolling in Medicare Parts A and B is mandatory to keep TRICARE for Life.
  • Medicare Part A (hospital) is generally premium-free if you have 40+ quarters of work credits.
  • Medicare Part B (medical) has a standard monthly premium of $202.90 in 2026, before any income-related surcharge.

How to Explain It

  • "When you turn 65, Medicare becomes your ticket to care outside the VA system. And if you want to keep TRICARE for Life, you MUST sign up for Medicare Parts A and B."
  • "Part A is usually free. Part B has a monthly premium. Some people balk at that cost, but military retirees need Part B to preserve TRICARE for Life."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "What happens if I miss the Medicare enrollment window?"

A: Your premiums go up permanently. For every 12-month period you delay Part B enrollment after turning 65 (without other qualifying coverage), your premium increases by 10%. This penalty lasts for as long as you have Part B.

Q: "Does VA healthcare count as 'other qualifying coverage' for Medicare?"

A: No. VA healthcare does not qualify as creditable coverage for Medicare enrollment penalty purposes. You should enroll in Medicare at 65 regardless of your VA enrollment.

Slide 5

TRICARE for Life

Key Facts & Numbers

  • TRICARE for Life is a wraparound secondary payer that works alongside Medicare.
  • Medicare pays first as primary insurer. TRICARE covers remaining costs as secondary.
  • Result: little to no out-of-pocket for most covered services.
  • Requirement: You must be enrolled in Medicare Parts A AND B to use TRICARE for Life.
  • No enrollment form is needed for TRICARE for Life specifically — it activates automatically once Medicare A and B are in place and DEERS records are current.

How to Explain It

  • "TRICARE for Life is one of the best benefits in the federal system. Medicare pays the bill, and TRICARE picks up everything Medicare doesn't cover. For most doctor visits and hospital stays, you pay nothing out of pocket."
  • "The key requirement is that you must have both Medicare Part A and Part B. If you skip Part B to save the premium, you can lose TRICARE coverage."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Do I need a TRICARE for Life card?"

A: There is no separate TFL card. You use your Medicare card at the provider, and TRICARE processes the secondary claim automatically. Make sure your information is current in DEERS.

Q: "Does TRICARE for Life cover dental and vision?"

A: Routine dental and vision generally need separate coverage, such as FEDVIP dental or vision plans if eligible, or VA dental benefits for veterans who qualify.

Slide 6

Medicare Part D & Enrollment Deadlines

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Part D (prescription drug coverage): VA drug coverage is comprehensive but requires VA provider authorization and VA pharmacies.
  • Medicare Part D gives you flexibility to fill prescriptions at any participating local pharmacy.
  • Missed enrollment: The General Enrollment Period (January 1 - March 31) allows late sign-up, but premiums may be permanently higher (1% penalty per month of delay).
  • TRICARE for Life acts as creditable drug coverage, so military retirees with TFL typically do not need Part D.

How to Explain It

  • "If you have TRICARE for Life, you probably don't need a separate Part D plan because TRICARE prescription coverage is creditable. VA drug coverage can also be creditable, but keep proof of that coverage."
  • "The Part B penalty and the TRICARE Part B requirement are the biggest Medicare-at-65 issues for this room. Part D is a separate drug coverage decision."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "I use the VA pharmacy for everything. Do I need Part D?"

A: Maybe not, because VA prescription coverage can be creditable. Consider Part D if you need local pharmacy flexibility, Extra Help, or medications that are inconvenient through VA channels. Confirm before deciding.

Slide 7

Coverage Comparison: VA vs. Medicare vs. TRICARE

Key Facts & Numbers

  • VA Healthcare: Best inside VA, with some authorized community, urgent, and emergency care. Copays vary by priority group.
  • Medicare: Any Medicare-accepting provider nationwide. Standard Part B premium is $202.90/month in 2026. No VA facility requirement.
  • TRICARE for Life: Wraps around Medicare after 65. Medicare pays first, TFL covers rest. Requires Medicare enrollment.
  • Best strategy: Enroll in VA healthcare, Medicare Parts A & B, and maintain TRICARE for Life. This gives broadest access with minimal out-of-pocket.

How to Explain It

  • "Think of it as three layers of protection. VA is your foundation — no premiums, familiar system. Medicare opens up the rest of the country. TRICARE for Life eliminates whatever Medicare doesn't cover."
  • "Having all three means you can see a VA doctor, a civilian doctor, or go to any emergency room and be covered. That's the gold standard."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "If I have all three, who pays for what?"

A: At a VA facility, the VA covers everything. At a civilian provider, Medicare pays first, then TRICARE for Life pays the remainder. Your out-of-pocket is typically zero for covered services outside the VA.

Q: "Is the Part B premium worth it?"

A: For military retirees, Part B is essential because it preserves TFL. For other veterans, the answer depends on civilian access needs, travel, income, and other coverage.

Slide 9

VA Aid & Attendance Overview

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Aid & Attendance (A&A) is a tax-free monthly enhanced VA pension for wartime veterans (or surviving spouses) who need help with Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) or require custodial care.
  • Care need examples: help with daily activities such as bathing, feeding, or dressing; being bedridden; being in a nursing home due to disability; or meeting VA's severe vision criteria.
  • This is NOT VA disability compensation. It is a pension benefit with income and asset limits.

How to Explain It

  • "Aid and Attendance is the VA's answer to long-term care costs. If you or your surviving spouse need help with basic daily activities — bathing, dressing, eating — this benefit can provide thousands of dollars a month, tax-free."
  • "It's a pension, not compensation. That means it's needs-based. There are income and asset limits."
  • Why it matters: "Long-term care costs $5,000-$10,000+ per month. A&A can cover a significant portion of that."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Can I get this even if I don't have a service-connected disability?"

A: Yes. A&A is based on wartime service and your current need for assistance, not on service-connected disabilities. You need 90 days of active duty with at least 1 day during a wartime period.

Q: "What counts as a wartime period?"

A: Major wartime periods include WWII, Korea, Vietnam (Feb 1961 - May 1975), Gulf War (Aug 1990 - present). Most students in this room served during a qualifying period.

Slide 10

2026 Aid & Attendance Rates

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Two veterans married to each other: $3,845/month
  • Married veteran: $2,874/month
  • Single veteran: $2,424/month
  • Surviving spouse: $1,558/month
  • Net worth limit: $163,699 (2026). Includes all assets except primary residence and personal belongings.
  • 3-year look-back period: The VA examines asset transfers made in the 3 years before applying. Transferring assets to qualify can result in a penalty period of ineligibility.

How to Explain It

  • "These are the 2026 monthly rates. A married veteran needing help with daily activities could receive almost $2,900 a month, tax-free. That's real money toward an in-home aide or assisted living."
  • "But there's a catch: you can't have more than about $164,000 in countable assets. And the VA looks back 3 years to make sure you didn't give away money just to qualify."
  • Emphasize surviving spouses: "If your spouse outlives you and needs care, she could receive over $1,500/month. Make sure she knows about this benefit."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Does my house count toward the net worth limit?"

A: No, your primary residence is excluded from the asset calculation. So is one vehicle. But savings, investments, and additional real estate do count.

Q: "Can I apply for my wife if I've passed away?"

A: Your wife can apply as a surviving spouse if you served during wartime. She would need to show she needs help with ADLs and meets the income/asset requirements.

Slide 11

Eligibility & Daily Help

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Service requirements: 90 days active duty, at least 1 day during a wartime period, non-dishonorable discharge.
  • Age/disability: Must be 65+ or permanently disabled.
  • Common Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) to document:
    1. Bathing
    2. Mobility / Transferring (getting in and out of bed or chair)
    3. Dressing
    4. Toileting
    5. Eating
  • VA's public eligibility language is broader than a fixed number of ADLs, so document all daily help, supervision, nursing home, bedridden, and vision-related needs.

How to Explain It

  • "The practical question is whether the veteran or survivor needs regular help to perform daily life safely. Do not reduce this to a single checklist number."
  • "Think about whether this applies to you now, or could in the future. And think about your spouse. These are the same criteria for surviving spouses."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "What if I need help with just one ADL?"

A: You may not qualify for the full A&A rate, but you might qualify for the Housebound benefit, which is a lower enhanced pension rate. A VSO can help you determine which level of benefit fits your situation.

Q: "Who determines whether I qualify?"

A: You file VA Form 21-2680 (Examination for Housebound Status or Need for Aid & Attendance), which is completed by your doctor. The VA then makes the determination.

Slide 12

Medicaid Interaction

Key Facts & Numbers

  • If a veteran receiving A&A is approved for Medicaid long-term care in a skilled nursing facility, the A&A benefit drops to approximately $90/month.
  • This is because Medicaid is a "payer of last resort" — once Medicaid covers nursing home costs, the VA reduces the pension.
  • This creates a planning dilemma: A&A can help pay for home care or assisted living, but if the veteran enters a Medicaid-funded nursing home, most of the benefit disappears.

How to Explain It

  • "Here's where it gets tricky. Aid and Attendance is great for paying for in-home care or assisted living. But the moment Medicaid starts paying for a nursing home, your A&A drops to about $90."
  • "This means you need to plan carefully. If there's a chance you'll need nursing home level care, think about the timing of Medicaid and A&A applications."
  • "This is absolutely a situation where you should consult an elder law attorney or VA-accredited financial planner before making decisions."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Can I use A&A to pay for assisted living and avoid Medicaid?"

A: Many people do exactly that. A&A combined with other income can cover assisted living costs for years. The key is planning ahead so you don't exhaust your assets and end up needing Medicaid for a nursing home.

Q: "What's the difference between assisted living and a skilled nursing facility?"

A: Assisted living provides help with daily activities but isn't a medical facility. Skilled nursing provides 24/7 medical care (nursing, physical therapy, etc.). Medicaid typically only covers skilled nursing, not assisted living.

Slide 2

Sequence of Returns Risk

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Sequence of returns risk = the danger of experiencing negative market returns early in retirement while you are withdrawing from your portfolio.
  • Two retirees with identical average returns over 20 years can have vastly different outcomes depending on WHEN the bad years happen.
  • A 15% drop in year 1 of retirement is far more damaging than the same drop in year 10, because you are selling shares at low prices to fund living expenses.

How to Explain It

  • "Imagine two people retire with $500,000. Over 20 years, the market averages 7% return for both. But Person A gets hit with a big loss in year one, while Person B gets the same loss in year fifteen. Person A runs out of money years earlier — even though the average was the same."
  • "This is why 'average returns' can be misleading. What matters is the ORDER of returns, especially in the first 5-10 years of retirement."
  • Why it matters: "If the market drops right after you retire and you're pulling money out, you lock in those losses. The portfolio never fully recovers."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "So should I move everything to safe investments before retiring?"

A: Not necessarily. Moving everything to safe investments protects against losses but means very low growth. That's where the bucket strategy comes in — it's a middle ground we'll cover next.

Q: "Is this why the G Fund is popular?"

A: Yes, many people move to the G Fund near retirement for safety. But staying entirely in G Fund for a 20-30 year retirement means inflation can erode your purchasing power. Balance is key.

Slide 3

The Three-Bucket Strategy

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Bucket 1 — Liquidity (1-5 years): Cash, CDs, money market. Covers near-term living expenses. No market risk.
  • Bucket 2 — Lifestyle (3-10 years): Bonds, balanced funds. Moderate growth with less volatility.
  • Bucket 3 — Legacy (10+ years): Stocks, growth assets. Higher risk but highest long-term growth potential.

How to Explain It

  • "Think of it like three containers. The first one is your wallet — money you need this month and the next few years. The second is your pantry — supplies for the medium term. The third is your savings for the long haul."
  • "When the market drops, you don't panic because your daily expenses come from Bucket 1, which isn't invested in stocks at all. The stocks in Bucket 3 have time to recover."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "How much should be in each bucket?"

A: A common starting point is 1-2 years of expenses in Bucket 1, 3-7 years in Bucket 2, and the rest in Bucket 3. But this depends on your total assets, guaranteed income (pension, VA), and risk tolerance. A financial advisor can help you size each bucket.

Q: "Can I do this inside my TSP?"

A: To some extent. The G Fund works like Bucket 1, the F Fund like Bucket 2, and C/S/I Funds like Bucket 3. The L Funds do something similar automatically. But for true bucket management, many people use a mix of TSP, IRA, and bank accounts.

Slide 4

How Replenishment Works

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Draw living expenses ONLY from the Liquidity bucket (Bucket 1).
  • Periodically shift funds from Lifestyle (Bucket 2) into Liquidity (Bucket 1) to replenish it.
  • The Legacy bucket (Bucket 3) is NEVER touched during market downturns.
  • In good market years, shift gains from Bucket 3 into Bucket 2 to refill the pipeline.

How to Explain It

  • "The system works like a waterfall. Money flows down from Bucket 3 to Bucket 2 to Bucket 1. You only spend from Bucket 1."
  • "When the market is up, you take some profits from Bucket 3 and move them into Bucket 2. When the market is down, you leave Bucket 3 alone and live off what's already in Buckets 1 and 2."
  • "The discipline is: never sell growth assets during a downturn. That's the whole point of having buckets."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "What if I run out of money in Buckets 1 and 2 during a long downturn?"

A: If you have 3-5 years of expenses in Buckets 1 and 2 combined, that covers most historical bear markets. The longest bear market since WWII lasted about 2.5 years. But this is why sizing the buckets properly matters — and why guaranteed income (pension, Social Security) is so valuable.

Slide 5

The 4% Rule & Safe Withdrawal Rates

Key Facts & Numbers

  • The 4% rule: withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one, then adjust that dollar amount for inflation each year.
  • Historically sustains a portfolio for 30 years (based on research by William Bengen, 1994).
  • $500,000 portfolio = $20,000/year ($1,667/month)
  • $750,000 portfolio = $30,000/year ($2,500/month)
  • $1,000,000 portfolio = $40,000/year ($3,333/month)
  • Caveat: The 4% rule is a guideline, not a guarantee. High inflation, low returns, or a very long retirement may require a more conservative rate of 3-3.5%.

How to Explain It

  • "The 4% rule gives you a ballpark for how much you can pull from your savings each year without running out. Take your total portfolio, multiply by 4%, and that's your sustainable annual withdrawal."
  • "But here's the important context for this room: most of you have pensions and VA benefits that cover a large portion of your expenses. The 4% rule applies to whatever gap your guaranteed income doesn't cover."
  • "If your pension and VA cover $4,000/month and you need $5,000, you only need to draw $1,000/month from savings. That's a much more conservative withdrawal rate."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Is 4% still considered safe with current inflation?"

A: Some financial planners now recommend 3-3.5% for added safety, especially for early retirees. The 4% rule was based on historical data that included both high and low inflation periods. Consult a financial advisor for your specific situation.

Q: "What if my portfolio is all in TSP G Fund?"

A: The 4% rule assumes a mixed portfolio of stocks and bonds. An all-G Fund portfolio has lower returns, which means 4% may be too aggressive. You might need to withdraw less or diversify your holdings. Again, a financial advisor can help run the numbers.

Slide 6

Income Stacking: Your Retirement Paycheck

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Most military retirees have multiple income streams:
    1. Military Retired Pay — Taxable, COLA-adjusted.
    2. VA Disability Compensation — Tax-free (may offset retired pay per Module 2).
    3. Social Security — Partially taxable (up to 85% can be taxed), begins at 62-70.
    4. TSP / IRA Withdrawals — Taxable (traditional) or tax-free (Roth).
    5. Employment Income — Second career, part-time, consulting.
  • Key insight: Understanding which streams are taxable vs. tax-free helps you optimize withdrawal order and minimize total tax burden.

How to Explain It

  • "Think of your retirement income like layers on a cake. Each layer has different tax treatment. The goal is to stack them in the right order so you keep as much as possible."
  • "General principle: spend your tax-free money last. Let Roth accounts grow. Draw from taxable accounts first if you're in a low bracket. This is called 'tax-efficient withdrawal ordering.'"
  • "But the specifics depend on your situation. A $50/hour consultation with a tax professional can save you thousands."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "How much of my Social Security is taxable?"

A: It depends on your total income. If your combined income (adjusted gross income + nontaxable interest + half of SS benefits) exceeds $25,000 (single) or $32,000 (married), up to 50% of SS benefits are taxable. Above $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (married), up to 85% is taxable. VA disability compensation does NOT count toward this threshold.

Q: "What order should I withdraw from my accounts?"

A: The conventional wisdom is: taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred (traditional TSP/IRA), then Roth last. But this is a general guideline. A tax professional can model the optimal order for your specific income mix.

Slide 8

Federal Taxation

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Taxable: Military retirement pensions are taxed as ordinary income at the federal level. They appear on your 1099-R from DFAS.
  • Tax-free: VA disability compensation is completely tax-free at both federal and state levels. It does not appear on any tax form.

How to Explain It

  • "Simple split: your military pension is taxed like a paycheck. Your VA disability is completely invisible to the IRS."
  • "This is why the CRDP vs. CRSC decision from Module 2 matters for taxes. CRDP is taxable, CRSC is not. More of your income in the tax-free column means more money in your pocket."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Can I adjust my withholding on retired pay?"

A: Yes. You can change your federal tax withholding through myPay by updating your W-4 (IRS Form W-4P for pensions). Review this annually, especially after any change in income or filing status.

Slide 9

State Tax Landscape (2026)

Key Facts & Numbers

  • 9 states with no state income tax (2026): Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, Wyoming.
  • New Hampshire joined this list in 2026 by eliminating its interest and dividends tax.
  • States that fully tax military retirement: California and a small number of others with no special exemptions for military pensions.
  • Many states fall in between — partial exemptions, age-based exemptions, or income thresholds.

How to Explain It

  • "Where you live in retirement can mean thousands of dollars a year in tax savings — or costs. Nine states don't tax income at all. Others give military retirees partial or full exemptions."
  • "California is the poster child for full taxation. If your military pension is $40,000/year and you're in the 9.3% bracket, that's $3,720 in state taxes. Move to Texas and that goes to zero."
  • "But — and this is important — taxes are just one piece of the puzzle. Property taxes, insurance, and cost of living matter too."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "What about the state we're in now?"

A: Check your specific state's rules. Many states have changed their military pension taxation in recent years (e.g., several states added exemptions between 2020-2026). The Military Officers Association of America (MOAA) maintains an updated state-by-state guide at MOAA.org.

Q: "If I move to Florida, does my state tax drop to zero immediately?"

A: For income earned after you establish residency, yes. You must actually establish legal residency (driver's license, voter registration, domicile). You cannot simply maintain an address there while living elsewhere.

Facilitation Tips

The scenario: MSG Rivera is retiring from Fort Liberty, NC. He is considering staying in North Carolina, moving to Texas (no income tax), or moving to Florida (near family). His pension is $3,200/month ($38,400/year).
Key factors to draw out:

  • Texas and Florida have no income tax, but Texas has high property taxes and Florida has high homeowners insurance.
  • Non-tax factors: VA facility access, spouse's employment, proximity to family, cost of living.
  • A $3,200/month pension in NC vs. TX might save ~$1,500-$2,000/year in state taxes, but property taxes could eat that up.
What good answers look like: Students weighing multiple factors, not just taxes. "He should check if there's a VA hospital near where he'd live in Florida" or "What does his wife do for work?"
Redirecting: If someone says "just move to a no-tax state," push back gently: "Is saving $2,000 a year worth living far from family? What about property taxes in Texas? Total cost of living is what matters."
Time: 5-7 minutes. Close with: "The lesson is: don't chase a tax break without looking at the whole picture."

DTI Impact & Hidden Costs

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Living in a state that exempts military retirement pay increases your net usable income, which lowers your Debt-to-Income (DTI) ratio.
  • A lower DTI means more VA home loan purchasing power.
  • Hidden costs warning: High property taxes (Texas averages ~1.8% of home value annually) and home insurance premiums (Florida averages $4,000+/year for homeowners insurance) can completely wipe out state income tax savings.
  • Always compare TOTAL cost of living, not just tax rates.

How to Explain It

  • "Here's a bonus benefit of living in a tax-free state: more of your income counts as 'usable' when applying for a VA home loan. Your DTI ratio looks better, which means you can qualify for a bigger mortgage."
  • "But watch out for the bait-and-switch. Florida has no income tax, but your homeowners insurance might cost $4,000-$6,000 a year. Texas has no income tax, but property taxes on a $300,000 house could be $5,400. Those 'savings' disappear fast."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "How do I compare total cost of living between two states?"

A: Use online cost-of-living calculators (NerdWallet, BankRate, or the Military Compensation Calculator). Look at housing, taxes, insurance, groceries, healthcare access, and transportation. The best comparison accounts for your specific income and spending pattern.

Slide 2

Survivor Benefit Plan (SBP)

Key Facts & Numbers

  • SBP pays 55% of your selected base amount to your surviving beneficiary for their lifetime.
  • Election is made at retirement via DD Form 2656.
  • It is a one-time, irrevocable decision (with rare congressional exceptions).
  • If the retiree declines SBP, the spouse must sign a notarized acknowledgment.
  • The base amount can be full retired pay or a reduced amount (minimum $300/month).

How to Explain It

  • "When you retire from the military, your pension stops when you die. Your spouse gets nothing — unless you elected SBP."
  • "SBP is essentially insurance that pays your spouse 55% of your retirement pay for the rest of their life. You pay a premium while you're alive, and in return, your spouse has a guaranteed income after you're gone."
  • "The decision is made once, at retirement. If you say no, your spouse has to sign a notarized form acknowledging they agreed. This is a big deal."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Can I elect SBP for someone other than my spouse?"

A: Yes, you can elect SBP for a former spouse (if required by court order), a child, or even an "insurable interest" person. But spouse coverage is the most common and generally the most valuable.

Q: "What if I'm not married at retirement?"

A: If you later marry, you can elect SBP for your new spouse within one year of marriage. This is one of the few exceptions to the irrevocability rule.

Slide 3

Premiums & Paid-Up Status

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Premium: 6.5% of selected base amount, deducted from pre-tax retired pay.
  • Paid-up status: Premiums stop after BOTH conditions are met: 360 payments (30 years) AND reaching age 70.
  • COLA: SBP payouts increase annually with the Cost of Living Adjustment — same as retired pay.
  • No medical exam: Unlike private life insurance, SBP enrollment requires no health screening. This is a major advantage for veterans with service-connected conditions who might be denied or overcharged for private insurance.

How to Explain It

  • "The premium is 6.5% of your base amount. So on a $3,000/month pension, that's $195/month. That comes out pre-tax, which reduces your taxable income slightly."
  • "The good news: you don't pay forever. Once you've made 360 payments AND turned 70 — both conditions — the premiums stop but the coverage continues."
  • "And unlike buying a life insurance policy at age 50 or 60, SBP doesn't require a physical. If you have health issues that would make private insurance expensive or impossible, SBP might be your best option."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "So if I retired at 40, I'd pay premiums for 30 years until age 70?"

A: Yes, exactly. Both conditions must be met. If you retired at 40, you'd make 360 payments by age 70 and both conditions would be satisfied simultaneously. If you retired at 45, you'd hit 360 payments at 75, but since you already turned 70, premiums would stop at 75.

Q: "Is the 6.5% worth it?"

A: It depends on your spouse's age, health, other income sources, and how long they might live. The break-even point is typically around 10-12 years of SBP payments to the survivor. If your spouse lives longer than that, SBP was the better deal compared to investing the premium yourself.

Slide 4

Pros of SBP

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Lifetime longevity insurance — pays no matter how long the beneficiary lives.
  • No medical exams or health-based denial.
  • Government-subsidized premiums (taxpayers share ~40% of the actuarial cost).
  • Inflation-indexed payouts (COLA) maintain real purchasing power over decades.
  • Premiums come from pre-tax income, reducing your taxable retired pay.

How to Explain It

  • "The biggest pro is longevity protection. Private life insurance pays a lump sum and when it's gone, it's gone. SBP pays for your spouse's entire lifetime, no matter how long that is."
  • "The government subsidizes about 40% of the true cost. You're getting a benefit that would cost much more on the private market, especially with the COLA adjustment."
  • "And unlike private insurance, nobody can deny you coverage because of your health. That matters a lot for veterans with disabilities."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "What does 'government-subsidized' mean exactly?"

A: The 6.5% premium you pay does not cover the full actuarial cost of the benefit. The federal government picks up the difference — estimated at about 40% of the total cost. This makes SBP significantly cheaper than an equivalent private annuity.

Slide 5

Cons of SBP

Key Facts & Numbers

  • No access to principal — cannot borrow against or cash out SBP.
  • If the beneficiary dies before the retiree, coverage stops with no refund of premiums paid.
  • SBP benefits may end if the surviving spouse remarries before age 55.
  • Less flexible than private term or whole life insurance.
  • Generally irrevocable — once elected or declined, changes are only possible during rare congressionally authorized Open Seasons.

How to Explain It

  • "Now the downsides. SBP is not flexible. You can't cash it out, borrow against it, or change your mind."
  • "The hardest part: if your spouse dies before you, all those premiums you paid are gone. There's no refund, no death benefit, nothing."
  • "And if your surviving spouse remarries before 55, the benefit stops. After 55, they keep it. This is worth knowing for younger spouses."
  • "These cons don't mean SBP is bad — they mean it's not the right choice for every situation. That's why we're looking at both sides."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "If my spouse remarries at 54 and then divorces at 56, do they get it back?"

A: If the remarriage that terminated the benefit ends (by death or divorce), the SBP benefit can be reinstated. Your spouse would need to contact DFAS to reinstate coverage.

Q: "Couldn't I just invest the 6.5% myself and do better?"

A: Possibly, if you're a disciplined investor and your spouse doesn't outlive the invested amount. But you'd need to account for inflation, market risk, and the possibility of a very long life. SBP eliminates that uncertainty. A financial advisor can model both scenarios for your specific situation.

Slide 7

Dependency & Indemnity Compensation (DIC)

Key Facts & Numbers

  • DIC is a separate, tax-free VA benefit paid to surviving spouses of veterans whose death was service-connected.
  • 2026 DIC rate: Approximately $1,612/month for surviving spouses (adjusted annually).
  • Critical change (January 1, 2023): The SBP-DIC offset was fully eliminated. Surviving spouses now receive BOTH their full SBP payment AND full DIC with no reduction.
  • This change makes SBP significantly more valuable for families where the veteran has a service-connected condition that could be linked to cause of death.

How to Explain It

  • "DIC is a separate benefit for surviving spouses when the veteran's death is connected to their military service. It's about $1,600/month, tax-free."
  • "Here's the big news: until 2023, DIC used to reduce SBP payments dollar-for-dollar. That offset is gone. Now your spouse can receive both in full. If you have SBP and a service-connected condition, this is a major financial improvement for your family."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "How does the VA determine if a death is service-connected?"

A: The surviving spouse files a claim with the VA (VA Form 21-534EZ). The VA reviews the veteran's service-connected conditions, medical records, and cause of death. If a service-connected condition contributed to the death, DIC is awarded. This can also apply if the veteran was 100% P&T (permanently and totally disabled) for 10+ years before death.

Q: "My spouse never filed for DIC. Is it too late?"

A: No. There is no deadline to file for DIC. However, if filed more than one year after the veteran's death, the effective date will be the filing date rather than the date of death. A VSO can help with the application.

Slide 9

Estate Planning Essentials

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Every veteran should have four documents in place, regardless of age or wealth:
    1. Last Will & Testament: Directs how assets are distributed after death.
    2. Durable Power of Attorney: Names someone to manage finances if you become incapacitated.
    3. Healthcare Power of Attorney: Names someone to make medical decisions if you cannot.
    4. Advance Directive / Living Will: Specifies end-of-life care wishes.
  • Free help: Many military legal assistance offices and VSOs offer free estate planning for veterans. VA medical centers can help with advance directives.

How to Explain It

  • "These are the four documents every veteran needs. Period. It doesn't matter if you have $500 or $500,000 in the bank."
  • "Without a will, the state decides who gets your assets. Without a power of attorney, your family may have to go to court to manage your finances if you're incapacitated. Without an advance directive, doctors and family members are left guessing about your wishes."
  • "The good news: these don't have to be expensive. Military legal assistance offices, VSOs, and VA medical centers can help with most of these at no cost."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Do I need a lawyer for a will?"

A: For a simple will, military legal assistance offices can help for free. For complex estates (multiple properties, blended families, trusts), an elder law attorney is worth the investment. Many offer flat-rate estate planning packages.

Q: "Is an advance directive the same as a DNR?"

A: Not exactly. An advance directive is broader — it covers your wishes for all kinds of medical care (feeding tubes, ventilators, pain management, etc.). A DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) is a specific medical order. Your advance directive might include DNR wishes, but it covers much more.

Q: "I did a will 20 years ago. Is it still valid?"

A: Probably, if nothing has changed. But if you've moved states, married, divorced, had children, or acquired significant assets, it should be reviewed and potentially updated. State laws vary on will requirements.

Slide 10

VA Burial Benefits

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Veterans with an honorable discharge are entitled to burial benefits at no cost to the family:
  • National Cemetery Burial: Gravesite, headstone, opening/closing of grave — all at no cost.
  • Headstone/Marker: Government-furnished for any eligible veteran, even in private cemeteries.
  • Burial Flag: U.S. flag to drape the casket, presented to next of kin.
  • Presidential Memorial Certificate: Signed by the sitting President.
  • Burial Allowance: Up to $2,000+ for service-connected death; $893 for non-service-connected (2026 rates).
  • Pre-planning: File VA Form 40-10007 to pre-arrange burial at a national cemetery.

How to Explain It

  • "Every honorably discharged veteran is entitled to these benefits. A national cemetery burial costs your family nothing — gravesite, headstone, everything is covered."
  • "Even if you choose a private cemetery, the VA will provide a headstone or marker at no cost."
  • "My recommendation: file VA Form 40-10007 now to pre-arrange your burial. It takes the burden off your family and ensures your wishes are documented."
  • "And make sure your family knows about these benefits. Too many families pay for services that the VA would have provided for free."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "Can my spouse be buried with me at a national cemetery?"

A: Yes. Spouses and dependent children can be buried in the same gravesite at a national cemetery at no cost. The spouse's name and dates are inscribed on the back of the headstone.

Q: "What about cremation?"

A: National cemeteries accommodate cremated remains. All the same benefits apply — gravesite, marker, flag, certificate.

Slide 11

Beneficiary Designation Checklist

Key Facts & Numbers

  • Critical rule: Beneficiary designations OVERRIDE your will. If your will says one thing and your beneficiary form says another, the form wins.
  • Review these annually and after any major life event (marriage, divorce, birth, death):
    1. SBP (DD Form 2656) — spouse, former spouse, or child.
    2. SGLI / VGLI — life insurance beneficiary (can be anyone).
    3. TSP (Form TSP-3) — Thrift Savings Plan beneficiary.
    4. VA Life Insurance — any VA-sponsored policy.
    5. Bank / Investment Accounts — transfer-on-death or payable-on-death designations.
    6. Retirement Accounts (IRAs) — primary and contingent beneficiaries.
  • Common mistake: Outdated beneficiaries after divorce or remarriage. An ex-spouse listed on SGLI or TSP WILL receive the benefit regardless of your will.

How to Explain It

  • "This is the most common estate planning mistake I see: people update their will but forget to update their beneficiary forms. Your will does NOT control who gets your SGLI, TSP, or bank accounts if there's a beneficiary on file."
  • "If you got divorced 10 years ago but your ex-spouse is still listed on your SGLI, she gets the money. Your current wife can have the best lawyer in the world, and it won't matter."
  • "Review every form on this list. Do it today. Do it every year."

Anticipated Questions

Q: "What if I can't remember who I listed?"

A: Log in to each account. TSP is at TSP.gov. SGLI/VGLI is through the VA. Bank accounts can be checked at the branch or online. If you cannot access old records, contact each institution directly and submit a new beneficiary form.

Q: "What's a contingent beneficiary?"

A: The backup. If your primary beneficiary dies before you (or at the same time), the contingent beneficiary receives the benefit. Always list one. Otherwise, the benefit goes to your estate and through probate.
