Module 1

Retirement Transition Timelines & Preparation

Marine Corps Transition (6 to 1 Months Out)

Key concepts include scheduling your final retirement physicals, executing a thorough job search strategy, and submitting Benefits Delivery at Discharge (BDD) claims within the critical 90-180 day window before separation.

  • Submit Benefits Delivery at Discharge (BDD) claims
  • Address Blended Retirement System (BRS) lump-sum options
  • Complete final outprocessing and relocation planning

Federal Employee Retirement (5 Years to 0 Months Out)

Tailored for both CSRS and FERS employees, this timeline emphasizes early preparation starting up to 5 years from your planned separation date.

Pro Tip: Calculate your expected annuity and TSP income early. Verify civilian/military service records and ensure all financial indebtedness is cleared well in advance of your final day.
Module 2

Navigating Disability Pay and Compensation

CRSC vs. CRDP

It is vital to understand the differences between Concurrent Retirement and Disability Pay (CRDP), which restores retired pay withheld due to VA offsets for service-connected disabilities, and Combat-Related Special Compensation (CRSC), a non-taxable compensation for combat-related disabilities. Retirees cannot receive both and must elect one annually.

The VA Waiver

By law, military retirees must waive a portion of their DoD retired pay dollar-for-dollar for their VA disability compensation. Changes in your VA disability ratings can trigger complicated retroactive debits and credits, which sometimes result in an unexpected debt owed to DFAS.

Interaction with Social Security

Your VA disability compensation is tax-free and does not count as "earned income" by the Social Security Administration, meaning it will not reduce or offset your Social Security retirement checks. The only exception lies in the needs-based VA Pension, which can be affected by other income.

Module 3

Healthcare & Long-Term Care Integration

Medicare, VA Benefits, and TRICARE

Veterans are strongly encouraged to enroll in Medicare Parts A and B at age 65, as VA benefits typically only cover care at VA facilities. For military retirees, enrolling in Medicare Parts A and B is mandatory to keep TRICARE for Life, which works as a powerful "wraparound" secondary payer program to Medicare.

The FEHB 5-Year Rule

Federal retirees can carry their Federal Employees Health Benefits (FEHB) and Federal Employees' Group Life Insurance (FEGLI) into retirement, but they must have been continuously enrolled in the program for the five years of service immediately preceding retirement.

VA Aid & Attendance Benefit

A crucial, tax-free monthly enhanced pension for war-era veterans and surviving spouses who need assistance with Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) or require custodial care.

  • Eligibility: 90 days active duty with at least one day during wartime
  • Asset Limits: Strict net worth limits and a 3-year look-back period on asset transfers
  • Coverage: Home care, assisted living, and skilled nursing
Module 4

Investment Risks, State Taxes & Buying Power

Sequence of Returns Risk

This is the risk of experiencing negative market returns late in your working years or early in retirement. Because you are withdrawing funds in a down market, it can permanently deplete a portfolio much faster than anticipated.

The "Bucket" Strategy

A mitigation strategy that divides assets into three buckets:

  • Liquidity: Cash for immediate 3-5 year needs.
  • Lifestyle: Diversified investments for years 3-10.
  • Legacy: Long-term assets for future growth.

State Taxation of Military Retirement

State tax policies vary dramatically. As of 2026, some states have no income tax (e.g., Florida, Texas), while others fully exempt military retirement (e.g., Arizona, South Carolina), and some fully tax it (e.g., California). Relocating to a tax-friendly state can boost your Debt-to-Income (DTI) ratio, significantly increasing your VA home loan purchasing power.

Hidden Costs Warning: Retirees must be wary of offsetting costs. High property taxes or massive home insurance rates can completely wipe out any state income tax savings you gained by relocating.
Module 5

Legacy Planning and Survivor Benefits

The Survivor Benefit Plan (SBP)

The SBP provides a guaranteed, inflation-indexed lifetime monthly payout to your surviving beneficiary. It is crucial to understand that declining SBP requires the spouse's notarized signature, and that the election is a one-time, irrevocable decision made at the time of retirement.

Pros of SBP

  • Protection against outliving your money (longevity insurance).
  • Automatic qualification without restrictive medical exams.
  • The costs are subsidized by the government.

Cons of SBP

  • No access to the underlying principal value.
  • Benefits abruptly end if the spouse predeceases the retiree (or sometimes upon remarriage before age 55).
  • Lack of flexibility compared to standard term or whole life insurance.

Recommendation: Conduct thorough financial modeling of insurable needs, income replacement strategies, and longevity risk to make the right choice for your family's unique situation before your retirement date.
